Session 01

What Is KeyCommand?

The AI command center, your role, and the one rule that never changes.

The Platform in One Sentence

KeyCommand is KeyPlayers' proprietary AI command center — a platform where AI agents draft every client deliverable, and a VA approves every output before it goes anywhere near the client.

KP currently runs 23 agents — the platform is built to support more as KP grows. Two kinds: an orchestrator that routes all work, and specialists that each handle one task. None of them act on their own.

The one rule that never changes: No agent sends, posts, publishes, or reaches a client without VA approval first. Not once. Not ever. You are the gate.

The Core Flow

Step Who Does It What Happens
1. Brief VA You write a brief and send it to the right agent via the Boardroom
2. Draft Agent (AI) The agent produces a draft output — it goes to the Operator Queue, not to the client
3. VA Review VA You review the draft in the Operator Queue: approve, revise, or reject
4. Client Review Client Approved outputs go to the client for final sign-off
5. Publish VA (or system) After client approval, the deliverable goes live

The Dashboard

When you log in, your dashboard gives you a live view of what's active, what's waiting for your approval, and what's in progress across the client's KeyCommand workspace.

Note for Trainers
Take a screenshot of the current KeyCommand dashboard and insert it here. Show trainees where to find the Operator Queue, the North Star Goal display, and current in-flight tasks. The dashboard UI is the most important thing for a first-week VA to recognize on sight.
The Human Margin: You Are Not Optional

KeyCommand is not automation. It is a human-in-the-loop system. The agents are faster than you at generating content — but they do not know the client the way you do. Your approvals are how you teach the system. Every approval you make is a data point. Every sloppy approval is a lesson in the wrong direction.

The Agent System

Orchestrators, specialists, and how to brief the right agent every time.

The 3 Tiers

Tier 1
Orchestrator — The Boardroom
Routes all briefs to the correct agent. Everything flows through here first. This is where you start every task.
↓
Tier 2
Executive / Org Chart Agents
AI CEO, COO, CRO, CMO — strategy-level agents that set direction, review priorities, and coordinate across the client's business. Use these for strategic questions and decisions.
↓
Tier 3
Specialist Agents
Each one does exactly one job. Reel Analyst, Content Cascade, Inbox Triage, Deliverable QA, Lead Research, and more — the agents you'll work with most on a daily basis.

Picking the Right Agent

Most briefs go to a Specialist. Use the Executive tier when the client needs strategic input, a directional decision, or a recommendation that touches the whole business. When in doubt, brief the Orchestrator and let it route.

Session 03

Writing a Brief That Works

The 6-part checklist that separates good output from useless output.

The Brief Checklist

Every brief you send to a KeyCommand agent should answer these 6 questions. If you skip one, the agent fills in the gap with a guess — and that guess may not match the client.

①
Client Name Which client is this for? Agents maintain separate context per client. Always specify.
②
Platform + Format Where is this going and what form should it take? (e.g., Instagram Reel script, email subject + body, LinkedIn post)
③
Topic or Input What is the content about? What raw material are you giving the agent to work with?
④
Tone or Voice How should it sound? Reference the client's existing content or the Agent Soul profile. (e.g., professional but conversational, direct, no jargon)
⑤
Constraints What must the output avoid? (e.g., no mention of competitor names, keep under 150 words, no emojis)
⑥
CTA or Goal What is the output supposed to make the reader do or feel? (e.g., book a call, reply to the email, click the link)

Good Brief vs. Bad Brief

Same client request. Different brief quality. Different results.

Bad Brief

"Write a LinkedIn post for the client about their new service."

Missing: which client, what service, who the audience is, what tone, what constraints, what the reader should do next. The agent will guess. The guess will be generic.

Good Brief

"Client: Marcus Reyes Consulting. Platform: LinkedIn post. Topic: new fractional CFO service launching next week for early-stage startups. Tone: confident, peer-level (not salesy). Constraint: no bullet points, no question hooks. CTA: reply to start a conversation."

The agent has everything it needs. The output will sound like Marcus, reach his audience, and drive the action he wants.

Your Turn — Drill 2: Write the Brief

A client sends you this voice memo (transcribed):

"Hey, can you put together something for Instagram about our fall cleanup offer? It's for homeowners, $299 flat rate, we run it every October. Keep it simple, we don't want it to look too salesy. And can you make sure to include our phone number at the end?"

Write the complete 6-part brief for the Content Cascade agent. Be specific. Do not leave any of the 6 fields blank.

Trainer note: Review each VA's brief out loud. Ask the group: "What would happen if field ③ was missing? What would the agent guess?"

Session 04

How KeyCommand Remembers

Knowledge Graph vs. Genes — two systems, two very different jobs.

KeyCommand has two memory systems that work together. Most VAs mistake them for the same thing. They are not — and understanding the difference changes how you operate.

System 1: The Knowledge Graph

The Knowledge Graph is KeyCommand's long-term memory database. Every time an agent completes a task, the relevant information — the output, the client context, the decisions made, the files used — gets stored in the Knowledge Graph.

Think of it as a living client file that grows every day. An agent briefed 6 months from now can pull context from that file: what tone the client preferred, what formats worked, what was previously rejected.

Knowledge Graph = the what. Past work, decisions, files, and context. The agent's reference library.

System 2: Genes

Genes are learned rules — built directly from your approval patterns. Every time you approve an output, KeyCommand notes what you approved and why. Over time, it builds a behavioral profile: this client prefers short sentences, this tone always gets approved, this format never makes it past you.

Genes are different from the Knowledge Graph because they're not about what the client needed — they're about how you respond as a VA. They adapt agent behavior based on your decisions.

Genes = the how. Learned patterns from your approvals. The agent's behavioral adaptation layer.
Knowledge Graph

Stores: past outputs, files, context, client history

Who builds it: agents (automatically)

Grows: every time a task completes

What it improves: relevance and context-awareness

Genes

Stores: learned rules from your approval decisions

Who builds it: you (through your approvals)

Grows: every time you approve or revise

What it improves: behavioral accuracy, output style, on-brief rate

What This Means for You

In your first 2–3 weeks, expect to do more corrections. The Genes layer is still learning your standards. By week 4–6, if you've been consistent, the agents will be hitting the brief faster with less revision from you.

The Consistency Rule
Inconsistent approvals = inconsistent output. If you approve a bullet-point format one day and revise it out the next, Genes cannot learn a stable pattern. You are the one responsible for the system getting smarter.
VA Responsibility: Build the Genes

After each approved output, add one Gene entry. Use specific language: "Client prefers sentence-form over bullet points in all email copy" or "Always include client's phone number in Instagram CTAs." One entry per approval session. This is not optional — it is how you get faster and more accurate over 30 days.

Session 05

The Approval Workflow

The Operator Queue, the 2-gate rule, and the 5 mistakes VAs make.

The Operator Queue

The Operator Queue is your primary workspace inside KeyCommand. Every agent output lands here before it goes anywhere. If the queue is empty, nothing is waiting. If the queue has items, your job is to review them before end of shift.

The queue is powered by Claude Opus 4.8. KP updates the model periodically as better versions become available.

The 2-Gate Rule

Gate Reviewer What You're Checking
Gate 1 — VA Review You Does this match the brief? Is the tone right? Are there errors? Would you send this yourself?
Gate 2 — Client Review Client Final approval before publish. The client sees only what passed Gate 1.

Only outputs that pass Gate 1 reach the client. You are the filter. The client's confidence in KeyCommand depends entirely on the quality of what you let through.

9 Questions Before You Approve

Before clicking approve on any Operator Queue item, run it through these questions:

  1. Does this match the brief I wrote?
  2. Is the tone consistent with this client's voice?
  3. Are all factual claims accurate and verifiable?
  4. Does it respect the client's constraints (word count, format, restrictions)?
  5. Is the CTA clear and correct?
  6. Is the platform formatting right (length, hashtags, line breaks)?
  7. Are there any spelling, grammar, or punctuation issues?
  8. Would I be comfortable with this going to the client right now?
  9. Is there anything here the client has previously told us to avoid?

The 5 Mistakes VAs Make

Common Mistake 1

Rubber-stamping. Approving outputs without reading them. This trains Genes in the wrong direction and destroys client trust fast. If you approved it, you own it.

Common Mistake 2

Briefing the wrong agent. A strategy brief sent to a Specialist agent, or an execution task sent to an Executive agent, produces misaligned output. Check your tier before you brief.

Common Mistake 3

Vague briefs. Missing tone, missing constraints, missing CTA. The agent fills the gaps with guesses. You get generic output. You revise. You lose time. The brief is the leverage point — invest in it.

Common Mistake 4

Ignoring the queue. Outputs sitting in the Operator Queue are not "in progress" — they are waiting for you. A queue that isn't cleared by end of shift delays the client and breaks the approval cycle.

Common Mistake 5

Not reading Executive Layer outputs before briefing Specialists. If an AI CEO has set a strategic direction, your Specialist briefs need to align with it. Check what the Executive agents have recommended before sending task-level briefs.

Session 06

Your Day in KeyCommand

A real Monday morning walk-through, start of shift to close-out.

Theory is only useful if you know what to actually do when you open KeyCommand on your first Monday with a client. Here is your full-day rhythm, broken into three phases.

Start of Shift

Orient Before You Brief

  • Open KeyCommand and check the dashboard — look at the North Star Goal and current priorities. Is anything urgent or overdue?
  • Check the Operator Queue — are there outputs from Scheduled Jobs or overnight tasks waiting for your review?
  • Check the client's Executive agent layer — has the AI CEO or CMO flagged any strategic direction updates overnight?
  • Based on what you see, brief your first agents for the day. Start with Inbox Triage if there are messages to handle.
During Shift

Brief, Review, Repeat

  • Send briefs to Specialist agents as tasks come in — one brief per task, one agent per brief.
  • Return to the Operator Queue as outputs arrive — don't let it pile up.
  • For each queue item: run the 9-question checklist. Approve, revise, or reject with a note explaining why.
  • If you reject, re-brief the agent with a more specific prompt and the reason for rejection.
  • For content deliverables, route approved items to the Content Lab for final packaging if needed.
  • Check Trend Radar if the client has a content brief — surface relevant trends before you brief the content agent.
End of Shift

Clear Before You Close

  • Clear the Operator Queue completely — nothing should be pending when you close.
  • Check Scheduled Jobs — are any automated tasks set for overnight or tomorrow morning? Are they configured correctly?
  • Update your Gene entries for the day — add one rule per significant approval or revision.
  • Send your EOD update to the client with: what was completed, what is in review, and what you need from them.
The Human Margin: The Pilot's Checklist

"The Command Center is the cockpit. The VA is the pilot." A pilot does not skip pre-flight checks because they're in a hurry. Your start-of-shift and end-of-shift routines are your pre-flight and post-flight. They are not optional. They are how you catch errors before the client does.

Your First Week Checklist

What to do on Day 1 with a real client before you brief a single agent.

Before You Open the Boardroom

Getting KeyCommand access does not mean you're ready to brief agents. Before you send a single brief, complete this checklist on Day 1 with a new client.

□
Get KeyCommand access for the client workspace Confirm with your KeyPlayers team lead that your login is active and scoped to the correct client account.
□
Locate the North Star Goal Find it on the dashboard. Read it. If it's missing or unclear, ask the client before doing anything else.
□
Review Agent Soul profiles for your top 3 agents Read the voice, values, and behavior profile for the agents you'll use most. Know their "personality" before you brief them.
□
Ask the client 3 onboarding questions (1) What format gets the best engagement in your content? (2) What topics or words should we always avoid? (3) Is there any existing content you love that we should match in tone?
□
Write your first Gene entry before briefing any agent Based on your onboarding conversation, add at least one rule to Genes before the first task. Start the learning layer with intent, not by accident.
□
Run your first brief through the Boardroom Choose a low-stakes task for your first brief (a single social post, not a campaign). Treat it as a test. Review the output carefully with all 9 checklist questions.

Day 9 — Key Takeaways

1. KeyCommand is human-in-the-loop. Agents draft. VAs approve. Nothing reaches the client without you.
2. Your brief is everything. A weak brief produces a weak output. Invest 3 extra minutes in the brief and save 30 minutes of revision.
3. Knowledge Graph stores what happened. Genes stores how you respond. They are two different systems.
4. Clear your queue every shift. An unchecked Operator Queue is a broken pipeline.
5. Consistency trains Genes. Inconsistent approvals teach the system nothing useful.
The Final Word

The Command Center is the cockpit. The VA is the pilot. Pilots do not blame the instruments when a flight goes wrong — they learn the instruments, trust the instruments, and fly with precision. That's your job in KeyCommand.
