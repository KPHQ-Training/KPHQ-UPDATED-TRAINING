Day 2 Review

Recap of prompting mastery before we cover the AI tool landscape — and how to pick the right tool for every task.

⏱ 10 min

Yesterday's Key Points

The 5 Building Blocks: Role, Task, Context, Constraints, Examples. Every great prompt uses most or all of these. Missing blocks = predictable failures.

Prompt Formulas: All-Purpose VA, Email Draft, Content Creation, SOP, Research Briefing, Meeting Recap. Copy the template, fill the brackets, run it.

Advanced Techniques: Chain of Thought (make AI think step by step), Few-Shot (give examples to teach the pattern), Iterative (refine across multiple rounds), Prompt Chaining (each output feeds the next).

Debugging Bad Output: 5 root causes — No Role, Vague Task, Missing Context, No Constraints, Task Too Big. Diagnose before you retry.

Golden rule: AI drafts. You decide. Prompting is your most transferable skill — it works on every tool you'll use today.

Quick Check — Do You Remember?

Q: Name the 5 building blocks.
Role, Task, Context, Constraints, Examples.

Q: What's Few-Shot prompting?
Including 2–3 examples in your prompt to teach AI the exact pattern, format, or style you want.

Q: AI gave you generic output. First diagnostic question?
"Did I give it a role?" If not, that's your first fix.

Q: When should you use Prompt Chaining?
When the deliverable is too complex for one prompt — break it into a sequence where each output feeds the next.

The AI Landscape

As of 2026, there are hundreds of AI tools. You don't need to know all of them. You need to know the categories — because categories stay stable even as individual tools change every few months.

⏱ 35 min
The key insight

Don't marry a tool — learn the category. ChatGPT will be replaced. Claude will be updated. Gemini will change. But "Chat AI" as a category will exist for a long time. Learn what each category does, and you'll always know which type of tool to reach for.

Category 1: Chat AI — General Purpose

These are conversational AI tools. You write a prompt, they respond. Great for writing, editing, summarizing, brainstorming, and most daily VA work. The primary tools in this category:

Claude (by Anthropic)
Primary KP Tool

What it's best at: Long documents, complex instructions, nuanced reasoning, precise output when you give detailed prompts. Claude handles 200,000 words in a single conversation — that's a full-length novel.

Why KP uses it: It follows multi-step, constrained prompts with fewer errors than competitors. It doesn't add things you didn't ask for. It stays on brief.

Current models (May 2026): Haiku 4.5 (fast, good), Sonnet 4.6 (daily driver — best balance), Opus 4.6 (most powerful, use for complex tasks).

Access: claude.ai (free tier available)
Paid plan: ~$20/month unlocks all models
Best for: Most VA tasks, long docs, precise output
ChatGPT (by OpenAI)
Chat AI

What it's best at: Creative writing, casual tone content, broad brainstorming. The largest user base and plugin ecosystem of any AI tool. GPT-4o is the standard model; o3 is the reasoning model for complex thinking tasks.

When to use it: When you need creative variety, when a client specifically uses it, or when you need plugins for specific integrations.

Access: chat.openai.com (free tier available)
Paid plan: ~$20/month for GPT-4o access
Best for: Creative content, brainstorming, variety
Gemini (by Google)
Chat AI

What it's best at: Tasks that live inside Google Workspace — Docs, Sheets, Gmail, Drive. Gemini 2.5 Pro is deeply integrated and can read your existing files, draft in Google Docs, and summarize email threads natively.

When to use it: When a client runs entirely in Google Workspace and wants AI that works inside their existing tools without copy-pasting.

Access: gemini.google.com (free with Google account)
Best for: Google Workspace-heavy clients

Category 2: Research AI

These tools search the live internet and show you where information comes from. Essential for anything where accuracy and sources matter.

Perplexity
Research AI

What it does: Searches the live web and summarizes results with cited sources. Every claim links to its original source. You can verify anything it tells you in one click.

The key difference from Chat AI: Chat AI knows what it learned during training (knowledge cutoff). Perplexity searches right now. For anything time-sensitive — competitor info, industry news, current prices — use Perplexity.

Important: Still verify what it finds, especially for numbers and statistics. Sources can be inaccurate too.

Access: perplexity.ai (free tier ~5 Pro searches/day)
Best for: Research with sources, competitor intel, current events

Category 3: AI Agents

Agents don't just answer — they act. They can browse websites, fill forms, run multi-step tasks, and complete workflows without you manually doing each step.

Manus
AI Agent

What it does: Manus can browse the web, interact with websites, run code, manage files, and complete multi-step tasks autonomously. You give it a goal; it figures out the steps and executes.

Example use case: "Go to these 5 competitor websites, find their pricing pages, and build me a comparison table." A regular Chat AI can't do this — it can't actually browse the web. Manus can.

When to use: Multi-step research, data gathering from multiple sources, tasks that would require you to open and interact with multiple websites.

Access: manus.im
Best for: Autonomous multi-step web tasks

Category 4: Video AI

HeyGen
Video AI

What it does: Creates talking-head videos from text. Choose a realistic AI avatar, write or paste a script, and HeyGen produces a professional video — no camera, no editing software, no recording.

Use cases: Client welcome videos, training content, product demos, video introductions, translated video content (HeyGen can translate and lip-sync to 40+ languages).

Access: heygen.com (free trial, limited minutes)
Best for: Video content without on-camera recording

Category 5: Coding AI

These tools write, debug, and execute code. As an AI Operator you likely won't use them directly — but you should know they exist so you can leverage them when needed and recommend them to technical stakeholders.

Claude Code  /  GitHub Copilot
Coding AI

Claude Code: A command-line tool that lets developers delegate coding tasks directly to Claude. It can read entire codebases, write new features, fix bugs, and run tests.

GitHub Copilot: Built into code editors — suggests code line by line as developers type, like autocomplete for programming.

Why this matters: If someone asks "Can AI help us build this feature?" — yes, tools like these exist. You now know what to point them to.

Use case: Know it exists, recommend it to technical stakeholders

Category 6: No-Code Automation

These tools connect your existing apps and automate repetitive tasks — without writing a single line of code. If you find yourself doing the same manual steps every day (copy from here, paste there, update this, send that), automation tools eliminate those steps entirely.

This is one of the most valuable skill categories in 2026. Anyone who can set up automations becomes exponentially more productive because the time saved compounds every single day.

Zapier
No-Code Automation

What it does: Connects 7,000+ apps and creates automated workflows called "Zaps." When something happens in App A, it triggers an action in App B — automatically, without you lifting a finger.

Example workflows:

  • New form submission → Create task in Notion → Send Slack notification to the team
  • New email with attachment → Save attachment to Google Drive → Log it in a spreadsheet
  • Calendar event starts in 15 min → Send a prep reminder to Slack with the meeting link and agenda
  • New lead in CRM → Send welcome email sequence → Create onboarding task → Notify the team lead

Why you should know this: Zapier is the glue between your tools. Anyone who can set up Zaps saves hours every week without even touching AI. This is one of the highest-value operational skills right now.

Access: zapier.com (free tier — 5 Zaps, 100 tasks/month)
Best for: Automating repetitive multi-app workflows
Make (formerly Integromat)
No-Code Automation

What it does: Similar to Zapier but with a visual workflow builder that shows your entire automation as a flowchart. More powerful for complex, multi-step workflows with branching logic (if this, then that — otherwise, do something else).

When to use Make over Zapier: When the workflow has conditions, loops, or more than 3 steps. Make handles complexity better and is often cheaper at scale. The visual builder also makes it easier to debug when something breaks.

Example: A lead submits a form → Make checks if they're a returning lead → If yes, route to existing account manager → If no, create new contact record → Send appropriate welcome sequence → Log everything to a master spreadsheet.

Access: make.com (free tier — 1,000 operations/month)
Best for: Complex multi-step automations with conditions
Airtable Automations
No-Code Automation

What it does: Airtable is a database/spreadsheet hybrid, and its built-in automations let you trigger actions based on record changes — send emails, update fields, post to Slack, run scripts — all inside the platform where your data already lives.

Example: A client tracking pipeline in Airtable. When a deal moves to "Won," the automation creates an onboarding checklist, sends a welcome email, notifies the team in Slack, and creates a Google Drive folder for the project — all without manual work.

Why it's different from Zapier/Make: Airtable automations live inside your database. You don't need to move data between tools — the trigger and the data are in the same place. Best for clients who already use Airtable as their central hub.

Access: airtable.com (free tier — limited automations)
Best for: Data-driven automations tied to a central database
Google Apps Script
Low-Code Automation

What it does: Adds custom automation to Google Sheets, Docs, Forms, and Gmail. You can build things like automated reports, custom form responses, email sequences triggered by spreadsheet changes, and dashboard updates — all running inside Google's ecosystem.

The AI Operator angle: You don't need to know how to code to use this. Claude (or ChatGPT) can write Apps Script code for you. You describe what you want ("When someone submits this Google Form, add their data to Sheet A, send them a confirmation email, and notify me in Slack"), and AI writes the script. You paste it in and click Run.

This is the bridge between no-code and code. You're not learning a programming language — you're using AI to write small, specific automations that live inside tools your client already uses.

Access: Free with any Google account
Best for: Custom automations inside Google Workspace

Automation Logic Fundamentals

Before you touch any automation tool, you need to understand the logic that powers all of them. Every automation — from a simple Zapier Zap to a complex Make scenario — follows the same structure:

Trigger → Condition → Action → Result

Trigger: The event that starts the workflow. "A new form is submitted," "An email arrives," "A calendar event starts in 15 minutes," "A spreadsheet row changes." No trigger = no automation.

Condition (optional): A filter that decides whether the workflow continues. "Only if the email is from a specific domain," "Only if the deal value is over $5,000," "Only if the form field says 'Urgent'." Conditions prevent your automation from firing on everything indiscriminately.

Action: What happens when the trigger fires and conditions are met. "Create a task in Notion," "Send a Slack message," "Add a row to a Google Sheet," "Send an email from a template." Most workflows have multiple actions in sequence.

Result: The end state. What does the world look like after this automation runs? A new lead is logged, a team member is notified, a report is generated, a follow-up is scheduled. Always define what "done" looks like before you build.

The AI Operator's stack

The most powerful workflows combine all three. Traditional automation handles the plumbing (schedule it, move it, file it). No-code connects the apps (when this happens there, do that here). AI handles the thinking (write it, analyse it, decide what it means). You — the AI Operator — design the system, set the quality bar, and handle the exceptions.

Building Automation Logic: 5 Patterns Every Operator Needs

Pattern 1: Intake → Process → Distribute

Data comes in from one source, gets processed, and goes out to multiple destinations.

Example — Event Registration: Attendee fills registration form → Airtable captures the data → Zapier sends confirmation email + adds to attendee spreadsheet + creates a Slack notification in #events channel + adds calendar hold for the organiser.

Where AI fits: If the registration includes a "What do you hope to learn?" field, Claude can analyse responses in bulk and generate a summary for the event organiser showing common themes.

Pattern 2: Monitor → Alert → Act

Continuously watch for a specific condition, notify the right person, and optionally take an initial action.

Example — Brand Mentions: Google Alert detects a new brand mention → Make checks if the mention is positive, negative, or neutral → Positive: logs to "Testimonials" sheet → Negative: sends urgent Slack alert to the marketing lead with a link and suggested response draft.

Where AI fits: Claude can perform the sentiment analysis that determines routing — something a simple keyword filter can't do reliably.

Pattern 3: Batch → Generate → Review

Collect items over time, then process them in one batch with AI.

Example — Weekly Content Pipeline: Content ideas accumulate in an Airtable base all week → Every Friday, Make pulls all ideas marked "Ready" → Sends them to Claude as a batch → Claude generates draft posts for each → Drafts land back in Airtable for human review and scheduling.

Key principle: AI generates, humans approve. The automation handles the logistics of moving content between tools.

Pattern 4: Transform → Reformat → Deliver

Take data in one format and convert it for a different audience or platform.

Example — Meeting Notes to Action Items: Zoom meeting ends → transcript is saved to Google Drive → Make sends transcript to Claude → Claude extracts action items with owners and deadlines → action items are created as tasks in Asana → summary is posted to the project Slack channel.

This is where AI Operators shine: The transformation step (messy transcript → clean action items) is impossible with traditional automation but trivial for AI.

Pattern 5: Collect → Analyse → Report

Aggregate data from multiple sources and generate a human-readable report.

Example — Weekly Business Dashboard: Zapier pulls data from Stripe (revenue), Google Analytics (traffic), Mailchimp (email stats), and Instagram (engagement) every Monday → All data lands in a Google Sheet → Claude reads the sheet and writes a 3-paragraph executive summary highlighting what went up, what went down, and what to focus on this week → Summary is emailed to the team.

Without AI: Someone manually writes that summary for 45 minutes every Monday. With AI + automation: it's generated and delivered before you open your laptop.

No-Code Automation Deep Dive: Core Concepts

If you're going to build automations — and as an AI Operator, you will — you need to understand these concepts regardless of which platform you use:

Data mapping: When data moves between apps, field names don't always match. "Full Name" in a Google Form might need to split into "First Name" and "Last Name" in your CRM. Understanding how data maps between tools is 70% of building automations that don't break.

Error handling: What happens when an automation fails? A good automation has a fallback: retry once, then send a Slack notification with the error details so you can fix it manually. A bad automation fails silently and you don't find out until a client asks why they never got their confirmation email.

Rate limits and quotas: Free tiers have limits (Zapier: 100 tasks/month, Make: 1,000 operations/month). Plan your automations knowing how often they'll fire. A workflow that triggers on every email will burn through limits fast; one that runs once daily is more sustainable.

Testing before deploying: Always run your automation with test data before letting it handle real work. Send yourself the test email. Check the test row in the spreadsheet. Verify the test Slack message. Never go live with an untested automation.

Documentation: Write a one-liner for every automation you build: "WHAT it does, WHEN it triggers, WHERE the data goes." Future you (or the person who inherits your work) will thank you. Store these in a central doc — your Automation Registry.

Privacy & Data Safety

VAs handle sensitive client information every day. Before you paste anything into an AI tool, you need to know what you're allowed to share — and what can go wrong if you don't.

⏱ 15 min
This is not optional

Privacy and data security is a professional responsibility. Mishandling client data — even unintentionally — can damage trust, breach contracts, and in some cases create legal issues. This section is mandatory knowledge for every VA at KeyPlayers HQ.

What Happens to What You Type Into AI?

When you type something into Claude, ChatGPT, or any AI tool, that text is sent to their servers. Here's what typically happens next:

🖥️
It's processed on their servers The text you input is used to generate the response. It passes through the company's infrastructure.
📊
It may be used to improve their model (on free plans) Many AI companies use conversation data from free-tier accounts to train future models. Paid plans typically offer the option to opt out of this.
👤
It's tied to your account Your conversation history is stored and associated with your account. Anyone with access to your account can see it.
🔒
It is not shared with other users Your conversations are private to your account. Other users cannot see what you typed. However, the company's staff and systems may access it.

What You Must NEVER Paste Into AI Tools

The following types of information should never go into an AI chatbot unless the company has specifically approved a secure, enterprise-level tool:

Type of DataExamplesWhy It's Risky
Personal identifiersSocial Security numbers, passport numbers, date of birthCould expose clients to identity theft
Financial dataBank account numbers, credit card numbers, revenue figures marked confidentialFinancial regulations may apply; breach of client trust
Passwords & login credentialsAny login details for client systemsSevere security risk — never paste these anywhere
Confidential contractsNDAs, partnership terms, pending dealsLegal exposure for the client if terms are leaked
Private health informationMedical records, health historyHIPAA and privacy laws apply
Unreleased business plansProduct launches, M&A activity, pricing strategyCould create competitive or legal problems

What's Safe to Use AI For

  • Drafting emails with placeholder names (e.g., "[Client Name]" instead of the real name)
  • Summarising public information or general industry content
  • Brainstorming ideas that don't reference specific client data
  • Creating templates and frameworks
  • Editing or rewriting copy that's already been approved for public use
  • Working with anonymised data where identifying details have been removed

The Safe Substitution Method

When you need AI to work on something that contains sensitive details, anonymise it first. Replace real names and details with placeholders, run the task, then fill in the real info manually afterward.

Example

Original (don't paste this): "My client Marcus Webb runs Pacific Realty Group, earns $2.3M/year and is in the middle of acquiring a competitor. Write a summary email."

Safe version: "My client [Real Estate Broker] runs a mid-size brokerage and is in an expansion phase. Write a summary email about their growth strategy." → Then replace [Real Estate Broker] manually with Marcus's name before sending.

Ask Before You Paste

When in doubt, ask yourself: "Would my client be comfortable seeing this in an AI company's training data?" If the answer is no — don't paste it. Use placeholders instead.

🔐
When working with enterprise clients: Ask whether they have an approved AI policy. Some companies restrict which AI tools their contractors can use. Always follow the client's guidelines — even if they're more conservative than these.

Claude Best Practices for AI Operators

Since Claude is the primary KP tool, here are the practices that separate amateur users from professionals. Build these habits now — they'll save you hundreds of hours over the next year.

1. Start every new client with a Project

Don't work in one-off conversations. Create a Project for each client. Upload their brand guide, sample emails, SOPs, and write clear Project Instructions. This is a 20-minute setup that saves you 5+ minutes on every single task going forward — because Claude already knows who the client is.

2. Use the right model for the right task

Sonnet 4.6 is your daily driver — use it for 90% of tasks (emails, content, summaries, SOPs). Opus 4.6 is for complex analysis, long documents, or anything where getting it wrong has consequences. Haiku 4.5 is for fast, repetitive tasks like classifying a list or quick rewrites.

Using Opus for a simple email wastes tokens and time. Using Haiku for a strategic recommendation wastes quality. Match the model to the stakes.

3. Never accept the first output

The first response is a draft, not a deliverable. Read it. Find what's close and what's off. Give specific feedback: "The intro is too formal — make it conversational" or "Cut the second paragraph entirely and expand the CTA." Professional prompters iterate 2–4 rounds on anything important.

4. Give Claude examples, not descriptions

When you want Claude to match a specific style, don't describe the style — paste an example of it. "Write like this email" is 10x more effective than "write in a professional but warm tone with some personality." Show, don't tell.

5. Use conversation history strategically

Claude remembers everything in the current conversation. Use this. Start with research ("Tell me the key themes in this document"), then build on it ("Now write an email highlighting themes 2 and 4"). Each message builds on the last — you don't need to re-explain context that's already in the conversation.

6. Break complex tasks into a chain

Don't ask Claude to "create a full content strategy with 30 posts and a calendar." Instead: (1) "Give me 5 content pillars for this client" → review → (2) "Using Pillar 2, generate 8 post ideas" → pick 3 → (3) "Write full posts for ideas 1, 4, and 6." Each step is reviewable. Errors get caught early.

7. Save your best prompts

Every time Claude produces excellent output, save the prompt that got you there. Build a "Prompt Library" — a doc or folder with your proven prompts organised by task type. Over time, this becomes one of your most valuable professional assets. You'll never start from scratch again.

8. Upload files instead of pasting text

When working with documents, upload the file directly to Claude rather than copying and pasting sections. Claude reads the full document, preserves formatting, and can reference specific parts. Pasting long text into the chat window often loses structure and context.

9. Tell Claude what NOT to do

Negative constraints are just as powerful as positive ones. "Do not start the email with 'I hope this finds you well.'" "Do not use buzzwords." "Do not add a summary at the end." "Do not invent statistics." These guardrails prevent the most common problems before they happen.

10. Verify facts, always

Claude will state incorrect information with complete confidence. Always check dates, names, statistics, URLs, and any factual claim before including it in client deliverables. Use Perplexity to verify anything time-sensitive. Your reputation depends on the accuracy of what you deliver, not the speed at which you produce it.

Exercise 1Tool Matching

For each scenario below, pick the best tool and explain why in one sentence. Then check your answers.

  1. "Can you find out what our top 3 competitors are charging right now?"
  2. "Write me a LinkedIn post about why hiring remote talent beats hiring local."
  3. "I need a 2-minute welcome video for new clients but I don't want to be on camera."
  4. "Summarise this 55-page partnership agreement and pull out the key obligations."
  5. "Go to these 6 websites, pull their team page info, and put it in a spreadsheet."
  6. "I want to draft emails inside Gmail without switching tabs."
  7. "Analyse this 3-month content calendar and tell me what's missing."
Show Answers
  1. Perplexity — live web search with verifiable sources. Chat AI only knows historical pricing.
  2. Claude or ChatGPT — straightforward writing task. Either works; use whichever you're more comfortable with.
  3. HeyGen — AI avatar video. Write the script, choose an avatar, done. No camera needed.
  4. Claude — best at handling long documents without losing context or needing to be split into chunks.
  5. Manus — multi-step browsing and data extraction. An agent that can interact with websites, not just read them.
  6. Gemini — native Google integration means it works inside Gmail and Docs without copy-pasting.
  7. Claude — excellent for analysis tasks where you upload a file and want structured, reasoned output.
Exercise 2Build Your Personal Tool Map

List the 5 tasks you do most often as a VA. For each one:

  1. Write the task
  2. Run it through the Decision Framework (4 questions)
  3. Identify the best tool
  4. Note any data safety considerations for this task

This becomes your personal cheat sheet — specific to how you actually work, not generic advice.

Exercise 3Data Safety Audit

Look at the last 3 things you prompted into an AI tool (or think of 3 common prompts you'd use). For each one, ask:

  • Does this include any personally identifiable information?
  • Does this include any financial or legal information marked confidential?
  • Would I be comfortable if the client saw this in a data review?

If any answer is "no" — rewrite the prompt using the Safe Substitution Method (replace real data with placeholders).

This is a habit to build now, not something to learn after a mistake.

Exercise 4Side-by-Side Comparison

Use the exact same prompt on Claude and ChatGPT:

You are a social media manager for a boutique fitness studio chain. Write a LinkedIn post about why group fitness studios should invest in community-building over paid ads. Tone: direct, smart, no corporate filler. Under 150 words. End with a question.

Compare the outputs. Which tool produced something closer to your ideal? What was different? Save both. This trains your intuition for which tool fits which task.

Exercise 5Automation Opportunity Finder

Think about your daily workflow. List every task you do that follows the same steps every time — the ones where you're basically a human copy-paste machine. For each one, answer:

  1. What triggers this task? (A new email? A form submission? A calendar event? A Slack message?)
  2. What are the exact steps you follow?
  3. Which apps are involved?
  4. Could Zapier or Make handle this without you? If yes — write the trigger → action flow.

The operators who earn the most are the ones who automate themselves out of repetitive work — so they can focus on the high-judgment tasks that actually require a human.

Exercise 6Claude Best Practice Application

Pick any task you've recently done with AI (or make one up). Write the prompt using at least 5 of the 10 Claude best practices from the Cheat Sheet. Specifically:

  • Include a role and clear task (Best Practice #1 mindset)
  • Add at least 2 negative constraints — things Claude should NOT do (Best Practice #9)
  • Paste or reference an example of what good output looks like (Best Practice #4)
  • Plan your iteration: before running it, write down what you'll check and how you'll refine (Best Practice #3)

Run the prompt. Then iterate at least once. Compare your final output against what you would have gotten without following the best practices.

Exercise 7Group Discussion: AI + Automation Brainstorm

In pairs or small groups, pick one of these real client scenarios and design a combined AI + Automation workflow:

  • Scenario A: A coaching business gets 15 new leads per week from a Facebook ad. Currently, someone manually emails each lead, adds them to a spreadsheet, and assigns a follow-up task. Design the automated version.
  • Scenario B: A real estate brokerage posts on LinkedIn 5 times a week. Currently, the VA writes each post from scratch, gets approval via email, then posts manually. Design the AI-assisted automated version.
  • Scenario C: An agency sends weekly client reports. Currently, the VA manually pulls data from 3 sources, writes a summary, and emails it. Design the streamlined version.

For each scenario: What does AI handle? What does automation handle? What still requires a human? Present your workflow to the group.
