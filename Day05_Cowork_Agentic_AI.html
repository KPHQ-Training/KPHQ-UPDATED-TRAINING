Three Claude models: Haiku 4.5 (fast), Sonnet 4.6 (daily driver — use for 90% of tasks), Opus 4.6 (complex analysis and long documents). Match the model to the task.

Claude Sonnet 4.6 — the daily driver. Fast, capable, handles most professional work.

What Is Cowork?

Cowork mode turns Claude from a chat interface into a hands-on teammate. It's the difference between AI that writes about your work and AI that does your work.

⏱ 30 min
The core difference

Claude.ai = You ask. Claude writes.

Cowork = You assign. Claude does — across your actual tools.

Cowork is a mode in the Claude desktop app (not the browser version) that gives Claude access to your files, connected apps, and browser. Instead of copy-pasting between tools, you describe what you need — and Claude works across your stack to make it happen.

How to Set Up Cowork

  1. 1
    Download the Claude desktop app.
    Go to claude.ai/download. Install the app for Mac or Windows. This is separate from the browser version.
  2. 2
    Open the app and look for Cowork mode.
    It appears in the sidebar or settings panel. Enable it to unlock the extended tool set.
  3. 3
    Connect your tools via plugins/connectors.
    In Cowork settings, you'll see available connectors — Slack, Gmail, Calendar, Notion, and others. Connect each tool you want Claude to access. You'll go through a standard permissions flow for each.
  4. 4
    Grant folder access when prompted.
    Claude can only access folders you explicitly choose. It will ask which folders to include. Start with your working folder — don't grant access to your entire system until you understand the tool.
  5. 5
    Test with a low-stakes task first.
    Before delegating anything important, run a test: "Read my last 5 Slack messages in [channel] and summarise them." This confirms the connection works and shows you what Claude can see.
Security note

Claude only accesses what you explicitly allow. It asks before sending messages, creating files, or taking any irreversible action. Every action shows up in the conversation — nothing happens invisibly.

If you're working with sensitive client tools, check your client's data policy before connecting. Some clients have restrictions on third-party tool access.

Skills and Plugins — Extending Cowork's Capabilities

Cowork isn't static — you can install skills and plugins that teach Claude new capabilities. Think of skills as specialized instruction sets that make Claude better at specific tasks.

Skills are focused instructions that Claude follows for specific types of work. Examples: a skill for creating Word documents with proper formatting, a skill for building Excel spreadsheets, a skill for evaluating SOPs. When you install a skill, Claude automatically uses it when the task matches.

Plugins are bundles of skills + tool connections. A design plugin might include skills for creating ads, reviewing designs, and connecting to Canva. Installing one plugin gives Claude multiple new capabilities at once.

Why this matters: The right skills turn Claude from a general-purpose assistant into a specialist. Someone handling content gets a content strategy skill. Someone managing operations gets an SOP evaluator skill. You customise Claude's expertise to match your work.

Common First-Time Setup Issues

Troubleshooting

"I can't find Cowork mode." Make sure you're using the Claude desktop app, not the browser. Cowork is a desktop-only feature. Download from claude.ai/download.

"My Slack/Gmail connection isn't working." Re-authorize the connection in settings. Some corporate accounts have restrictions that block third-party access — check with your IT or the client's IT team.

"Claude can't see my files." You need to explicitly grant folder access. Claude doesn't have access to your entire computer — only the folders you select. Go to Cowork settings and add the folders you want Claude to access.

"Claude is asking for permission on everything." That's by design. It confirms before any action that creates, sends, or modifies something. This is a safety feature, not a bug. Over time, you'll learn which approvals are routine and which need careful review.

Combining Cowork with No-Code Automation

Cowork handles the thinking and creating. No-code automation handles the repeating and routing. When you combine them, you build workflows that run themselves — with your oversight at the right checkpoints.

The AI + Automation Stack

Layer 1 — Claude (Cowork): Reads, analyses, drafts, decides. Anything that requires understanding context, generating language, or making judgment calls.

Layer 2 — No-Code Automation (Zapier / Make / Airtable): Routes, triggers, schedules, distributes. Anything that follows the same pattern every time — "when X happens, do Y."

Layer 3 — You: Reviews, approves, escalates, quality-checks. The human in the loop who ensures nothing goes out that shouldn't.

Example: Automated Content Pipeline

5. Post goes live on scheduleAutomationLinkedIn / Instagram / X

You went from "write one post, copy it, open the scheduler, paste it, set the date, repeat 10 times" to "review a spreadsheet and check some boxes." That's the difference between working harder and working smarter.

Agentic AI

The shift from "AI that responds" to "AI that acts." Understanding this distinction prepares you for how AI is actually being used in professional environments in 2026.

⏱ 30 min

What Makes AI "Agentic"?

Regular AI waits for a prompt and gives a response. You evaluate it, then decide what to do next. Agentic AI breaks down a goal into steps, chooses which tools to use, executes across multiple systems, and reports back — all from one instruction.

Level 1
Chatbot
Answers questions. One output per prompt.
"What's a good subject line for this email?"
Level 2
Assistant
Completes tasks. Produces usable output.
"Write the full email with that subject line."
Level 3
Agent
Orchestrates multi-step workflows across tools.
"Find the lead, draft the email, check the calendar for a follow-up slot, and send a reminder."

Claude in Cowork mode operates at Level 2.5–3 — it can complete multi-step tasks across real tools when you give it a clear goal. This is what separates it from Claude.ai in the browser.

Practical Agentic Patterns

Pattern 1: Research → Synthesise → Deliver

Claude gathers information from multiple sources (files, Slack, email, web), synthesises it, and delivers a formatted output — without you jumping between tabs.

Pattern 2: Inbox → Triage → Draft → Queue

Claude reads new emails, categorises by priority, drafts responses in the client's voice, and queues them for your review — all in sequence, from one prompt.

Pattern 3: Brief → Plan → Create → Format

You give Claude a content brief. It uses the Project Knowledge to generate a plan, writes the content, and formats it per platform — LinkedIn post, email newsletter, and Instagram caption — from a single instruction.

More Agentic Patterns

You'll recognise some of these from Day 3's automation patterns — the difference is that in an agentic context, Claude handles the judgment calls, not just the routing.

Pattern 4: Monitor → Alert → Act

You set a standing instruction. Claude monitors a source (inbox, Slack channel, task board) at a scheduled interval and only surfaces what needs your attention. Think of it as a smart filter that understands priority.

ExampleEvery morning at 8am, check #client-updates for any messages from the past 18 hours. If there's anything that mentions a deadline, a blocker, or a client complaint, summarise it and send me a Slack DM. Otherwise, just say "Nothing urgent in #client-updates."

Pattern 5: Transform → Reformat → Distribute

Claude takes content in one format and transforms it for multiple destinations. One input, many outputs — each tailored to its platform.

ExampleHere's the transcript from Mitch's 12-minute YouTube video about hiring VAs. Transform into: 1. A LinkedIn post (200 words, insight-led, end with a question) 2. 3 Twitter/X posts (each under 280 chars, punchy, standalone) 3. An email newsletter intro (150 words, personal tone, one CTA) 4. 5 Instagram carousel slides (each slide = one key point, under 30 words) Use KP's brand voice from the Project Knowledge.

Pattern 6: Audit → Score → Recommend

Claude reviews a process, document, or system against criteria you define, scores each component, and gives specific recommendations. This is how you use AI for quality assurance, not just content creation.

ExampleReview this SOP for client onboarding. For each step, score 1-5 on: - Clarity (could a new VA follow this without asking questions?) - Completeness (are all edge cases covered?) - Automation potential (could this step be automated?) Flag any step scoring below 3 and suggest a specific fix.

Oversight & Responsible Use

The more powerful the tool, the more important your judgment becomes. Agentic AI is remarkably powerful — and that means knowing when to let it run and when to stay in the loop.

⏱ 25 min
The VA's role doesn't disappear — it evolves

AI that can act doesn't replace your judgment. It amplifies it. When Claude drafts 15 emails in 3 minutes, your job is still to read each one and decide which to send. The volume of output increases — your responsibility to review it doesn't decrease.

The most professional VAs using agentic AI are the ones with the best QA instincts, not the ones who delegate and disappear.

Calibrating Your Level of Oversight

Not every task needs the same level of review. Here's how to think about it:

Brainstorming, drafts, frameworks, internal notes
Light review
Client emails, social posts, summaries for client review
Standard review
Sending on behalf of client, publishing content, filing documents
Full review + approval
Any action involving money, legal documents, or external commitments
Human decision required

When NOT to Use Agentic AI

  • When the stakes of an error are high and irreversible. Sending the wrong email to 500 people, deleting a folder, making a purchase — these require human judgment, not AI speed.
  • When the context is too sensitive for AI to handle. Crisis communications, client conflicts, legal matters, anything emotionally charged. AI doesn't understand stakes the way people do.
  • When you don't understand what it's doing. If Claude takes an action and you're not sure why, stop and review before letting it continue. Understanding the process is part of your job.
  • When the client hasn't approved AI in their workflow. Always check. Some clients explicitly don't want AI handling their communications, even behind the scenes.

The "Would I Sign This?" Test

Before you send, post, file, or deliver anything AI has produced, ask: Would I put my name on this and stand behind it?

If yes — it's ready. If no — it needs more work. Your professional reputation is attached to every output that leaves your hands, regardless of who (or what) helped create it.

The professional standard

Clients hired you. They trust your judgment, your communication, and your standards. AI is your tool. You are responsible for its output. Never let speed become an excuse for skipping the review that protects your client — and your reputation.

Client Disclosure — When to Tell Clients You Use AI

This is a real professional question with no universal answer. Here's a framework:

Always disclose when the client explicitly asks, when AI is generating content they'll publish under their name, or when your contract or NDA addresses AI tool usage. Transparency builds trust.

Disclose proactively when you're using AI to handle their emails, access their tools, or process their data. They have a right to know what tools are touching their information.

Disclosure isn't needed for your own internal efficiency — using AI to brainstorm, organise your own tasks, or research publicly available information. You don't disclose every tool you use; AI is one of many.

Our position: We're transparent about being AI-enabled. It's a selling point, not a secret. We use AI to deliver better, faster work — and clients benefit from that. Frame it as a capability, not a confession.

Data Privacy Checklist for AI-Enabled VAs

Before running any AI workflow that touches client data, run through this checklist:

Pre-flight checklist

1. What data am I giving to AI? If it includes names, financial info, medical data, legal docs, or login credentials — stop. Use the Safe Substitution Method (replace with placeholders).

2. Does the client know AI is involved? If they haven't been told and you're processing their proprietary data, pause and have that conversation first.

3. Am I using the right account? Client work should go through a dedicated VA account, not your personal Claude account. Keep client data separate from personal data.

4. Is this reversible? Before Claude sends an email, posts content, or deletes a file — can you undo it if something goes wrong? If not, double your review time.

5. Would I be comfortable if the client saw this conversation? If the answer is no, reconsider what you're sharing with AI.

Common Mistakes VAs Make With Agentic AI

Learn from others' mistakes

"I let Claude send it without reading it." A VA approved an email draft without review. Claude had included a made-up statistic. The client forwarded it to their board. Always read before approving send.

"I automated too early." Setting up a Zapier workflow before understanding the process manually means you automate mistakes. Learn the workflow by hand first, then automate once it's reliable.

"I connected everything at once." A VA connected Slack, Gmail, Calendar, and files all in one session, then got overwhelmed by what Claude could access. Start with one connection. Learn it. Then add the next.

"I forgot to check the output on different platforms." Content that looks right in Claude doesn't always look right when pasted into LinkedIn or Instagram. Always preview on the destination platform before scheduling.
