Day 8 · AI Note-Takers

AI Meeting Note-Takers

Meetings are where decisions get made -- and where action items get forgotten. AI note-takers capture everything so you don't have to scramble. For a VA, this is one of the highest-leverage tools you can offer a client.

What an AI note-taker does

It joins a meeting (Zoom, Google Meet, Teams), records and transcribes everything spoken, then produces a clean summary with key decisions, action items, and who's responsible -- automatically, within minutes of the call ending.

The Main Tools

ToolKnown ForBest For
FathomFree, fast, clean summaries; great Zoom/Meet integrationVAs & small teams wanting zero-cost, high quality
Otter.aiLive transcription, searchable archiveDetailed word-for-word records
Fireflies.aiStrong CRM/automation integrations, searchable "knowledge base"Teams that pipe notes into other tools
Zoom AI CompanionBuilt into Zoom -- no extra bot to addClients who live in Zoom already
tl;dvTimestamped highlights & clipsReviewing key moments by video
Where to start

Most VAs start with Fathom -- it's free, reliable, and produces excellent summaries with action items already separated out. Learn one tool deeply before worrying about the rest. The skill transfers; the buttons just move.

AI Note-Takers · Quick Drill

Check Your Understanding

Tool Selection
A client runs all their meetings on Zoom and wants a free, minimal-setup AI note-taker. Which tool do you recommend first?
Fathom is the right starting point. It's free, integrates natively with Zoom, and produces clean summaries with action items already separated. Fireflies is better for teams that need CRM integrations. tl;dv is built for reviewing video clips -- not the quick action-item output a solo client needs.
Consent Scenario
A client asks you to add an AI note-taker to their upcoming call with an employment lawyer about a staff dispute. What is the right move?
Legal, HR, and medical calls require extra care. "The client said yes" is not enough -- recording laws vary by region, and every participant has rights. Always get all-party consent confirmed before recording sensitive calls. When in doubt, skip the bot and take manual notes instead.

How It Works, Step by Step

1
Connect & admit the notetaker

Link the tool to the client's calendar. It auto-joins scheduled calls, or you add the bot manually. It appears as a participant.

2
It records & transcribes live

During the call it captures audio and builds a full transcript in real time.

3
It generates a summary

Minutes after the call: a recap, key points, decisions, and a clean action-item list with owners.

4
You review & route

The VA's job: verify accuracy, then send the summary where it belongs -- a PM tool, Slack, email, or the client's inbox.

The hallucination trap

AI note summaries sometimes invent action items or misattribute who said what. Always cross-check the action list against what was actually said before you send tasks to people. A made-up task assigned to a real person damages trust fast. The notetaker and Claude draft; you are the fact-checker.

Claude as Your Meeting Processor

Drop a transcript into Claude (a Project for that client gives it voice and context) and it can produce exactly the output you need:

Meeting Processor Prompt
Here's the transcript from [client]'s team call.

Produce four things:
1. A 5-bullet executive summary
2. KEY DECISIONS made (with who decided)
3. ACTION ITEMS as a table: Task | Owner | Due Date | Priority
4. OPEN QUESTIONS that still need answers

Only include action items that were actually stated -- do not invent tasks.
Flag anything ambiguous as "needs confirmation."

Notes to Action · Quick Drill

Spot the Hallucination

This is a core VA skill. Claude processed the transcript below and produced four action items. One was never actually stated in the meeting. Which one?

Meeting transcript summary

Alex confirmed he'll send the updated campaign brief to the team by Thursday. The group agreed to delay the podcast launch by two weeks to allow more prep time. Maria offered to loop in the design team once the brief is finalized.

Which item did nobody actually say?
Claude's action items from the above call -- identify the hallucination.
Nobody mentioned a strategy retreat. Claude inferred something plausible-sounding but completely unsupported by the transcript -- a textbook hallucination. The other three were stated explicitly. Day 8 · PM Tools

Project Management Tools

PM tools are where work gets tracked. As a VA you'll live in whichever one your client uses -- so you need to be fluent in the concepts that all of them share, and know how AI plugs in.

The Major Tools

ToolPersonalityBest Fit
ClickUpPowerful, highly customizable, does everythingTeams wanting one tool for all work
AsanaClean, task-focused, easy to learnMarketing & ops teams
TrelloSimple Kanban boards, very visualSmall teams, simple workflows
Monday.comColorful, spreadsheet-style, flexibleVisual teams who like dashboards
NotionDocs + databases + tasks in one workspaceKnowledge-heavy teams & wikis
The Insight

Don't get overwhelmed by the choices. They all share the same core ideas: tasks, assignees, due dates, statuses, and boards/lists. Learn those concepts once and you can work in any PM tool within an hour. The differences are mostly cosmetic.

The Universal Concepts

Always the same
Task (a unit of work), Assignee (who owns it), Due date, Status (To Do / Doing / Done), Board or List (the container).
Just different names
ClickUp "Spaces", Asana "Projects", Trello "Boards", Notion "Databases", Monday "Boards". Same idea, different label. Don't let the vocabulary intimidate you.

AI + PM Tools

This is where the meeting pipeline pays off. With Cowork or an MCP connection (Days 4--5), Claude can work directly in the PM tool:

  • Create tasks from a transcript -- "Add these 6 action items to Asana with owners and due dates"
  • Check status -- "What's overdue in the client workspace?"
  • Summarize progress -- "Write a weekly progress update from this board"
  • Triage & organize -- "Sort these new tasks by priority and assign owners"
Try This (Cowork / MCP)
From the action items we just verified, create tasks in [PM tool] under the
"[Project]" board. For each: set the owner, a realistic due date, and priority.
Then give me a list of what you created so I can double-check before it's final.
The Rule

Every tool here drafts, captures, or routes. Not one of them decides. That's still you. An accurate board is a reflection of human judgment -- not AI output.

Day 8 · Messaging Automations

Messaging Automations

Slack, WhatsApp, and Telegram are where teams and customers actually talk. Connecting them to your AI stack means the right message reaches the right place automatically -- no manual copy-paste, nothing missed.

The Three Platforms -- and What They're For

Slack -- internal team comms

The team's HQ. Used for internal coordination, alerts, and updates. As a VA you'll route notifications here: hot-lead alerts, meeting recaps, daily summaries, task updates.

Automation examples: "New high-value lead in GHL -- post to #sales." "Meeting ended -- post the recap to #team." "Task marked done -- notify the owner."

WhatsApp -- customer conversations

Often the main channel to customers, especially outside the US. Via GHL's WhatsApp integration or the Business API, you can automate confirmations, reminders, and 2-way support.

Automation examples: appointment confirmations, order updates, and follow-up sequences -- with a human stepping in for anything nuanced.

Telegram -- alerts & bots

Developer-friendly and great for instant notifications via bots. Popular for personal alert channels and lightweight community groups.

Automation examples: a bot that pings the owner the second a big deal closes, or a daily-metrics bot that posts numbers every morning.

How These Connect to Your Stack

You rarely connect these by hand-coding. You use the connectors from Day 7:

tl;dv · Step 3

Route the Output — Your VA Workflow

The email is the source. You are the router. Here is what to do with it after every meeting.

1
Read the summary and action items

Don't forward anything until you've read it. Check the action items against the transcript. Did tl;dv invent anything? Remove it before it reaches anyone.

2
Paste into Claude for structured output

Use the Meeting Processor prompt from Session 2. Claude reformats the action items into a clean table (Task | Owner | Due | Priority) ready to drop into any PM tool.

3
Route each piece to the right destination

Action items → PM tool. Quick recap → Slack or team channel. Client-facing summary → your own words, not a raw AI dump. Sales follow-ups → GHL.

4
Label tl;dv emails in Gmail (one-time setup)

Gmail → Settings → Filters → Create new filter → From: tldv.io → Apply label "Meeting Recaps." Every future summary is auto-organized, not buried.

The complete free pipeline — no paid tools required

tl;dv free records + transcribes → email delivers the summary → you verify + route → Claude free structures it → Trello/Notion free tracks the tasks. Zero subscription cost for the VA side of this workflow.

tl;dv · Quick Drill

QA Checklist + Free Tier Scenario

Run this checklist every time before you forward a tl;dv summary to a client or team.

6-point QA checklist
  1. Consent announced — all participants knew they were being recorded before the call started
  2. Tested with a dummy meeting first — never a live client call for setup testing
  3. Summary email arrived — check spam if it's been more than 10 minutes
  4. Action items verified against the transcript — nothing invented or misattributed
  5. Speaker names and owners correct — tl;dv sometimes misidentifies who said what
  6. Forwarded in your own words — not a raw AI output pasted directly to the client

Always have a fallback — that's the VA standard.

Genes -- KeyCommand's Memory Layer

Genes is the adaptive memory system built into KeyCommand. Every brief you write, every output you approve, and every correction you make trains it. The more you use it, the smarter your agents get.

What Genes stores

Genes builds a running memory profile for each client: their tone and voice, content formats that land, brief structures that produce good output, and what you approve vs. send back for rework. It's per-client -- what it learns for one account stays in that account.

You don't configure it. It runs in the background and surfaces what it's learned through progressively tighter agent output over time.

KeyCommand · Quick Drill

Each Specialist does one job. Briefing the wrong one wastes time and produces useless output.

Reel Analyst is the competitor teardown agent. Give it the competitor's handle or reel URL and it returns the hook structure, key phrases, and winning angle. Content Cascade is purpose-built for this -- one approved pillar piece becomes five platform-native drafts in one brief. Content Writer creates new posts from scratch. Carousel Generator is specifically for the multi-slide carousel format, not cross-platform distribution.
