Day 7 · Session 2

Workflow Fundamentals

A workflow is GoHighLevel's automation builder. Once you understand its three moving parts, you can read and build almost any automation a client needs.

The whole concept in one sentence

A workflow says: "WHEN this happens (trigger), DO these things (actions), sometimes only IF certain conditions are met."

The Three Building Blocks

1
Trigger: the "when"

The event that starts the workflow. Nothing happens until a trigger fires. Common triggers:

  • A tag is added (e.g. new-lead)
  • A form or survey is submitted
  • An appointment is booked, or status changes
  • A contact replies to a message
  • An opportunity moves to a new stage
2
Actions: the "do"

What the workflow does once triggered. Actions run in order, top to bottom. Common actions:

  • Send an SMS or email
  • Wait (a delay, e.g. "wait 1 day")
  • Add or remove a tag
  • Create or move an opportunity
  • Notify the team / assign a task
  • Send data to another tool (webhook)
3
Conditions: the "only if"

Logic that branches the path. "If/else" splits let a workflow behave differently for different contacts, e.g. if the lead booked a call, send a confirmation; else, keep following up. Conditions are what make automations feel smart instead of robotic.

How to Build One

1
Automation → Workflows → Create Workflow

Start from a recipe template or a blank workflow.

2
Add your trigger

Click "Add New Trigger," choose the event, and set its filters (e.g. only when source = Facebook).

3
Add actions in order

Click the + to add each step. Use Wait steps to space messages out so they feel human.

4
Test before publishing

Enroll your own test contact and watch it run. Confirm every message and tag fires correctly.

5
Publish & monitor

Toggle the workflow live. Check the enrollment history daily for the first week to catch anything odd.

The #1 automation mistake

Never publish a workflow without testing it on a fake contact first. A broken automation doesn't fail quietly. It can text hundreds of real leads the wrong message, at 3am, on repeat. Test, watch it run end-to-end, then go live. (Remember Day 5: "I automated too early" is a real and costly mistake.)

Day 7 · Session 3

Reading the Logic, Not Just the Steps

A workflow with conditions branches into more than one path. The skill isn't memorising every block. It's reading the shape of the logic so you can predict what happens to any given contact.

Read it like a flowchart

Ask three questions for any workflow: (1) What starts it? (the trigger), (2) Where can it branch? (every if/else), (3) Where does each path end? A contact either reaches the end, gets handed to another workflow via a tag, or sits in a wait step. If you can answer all three, you understand the automation.

Day 7 · Session 5

Bulk Campaigns vs. Workflows

Before moving past automations, settle one mix-up that trips up almost every new VA: a Campaign and a Workflow look similar but solve opposite problems.

Workflow, ongoing & triggered
Runs continuously in the background. Each contact enters on their own schedule, whenever their trigger fires. Built once, runs forever (recipes 1-7 are all workflows).
Campaign, one-off & scheduled
A single broadcast sent once to a chosen list, e.g. "email everyone on the November newsletter list this Friday at 9am." Everyone gets it at the same time, then it's done.

How to Decide

If the request is…Use…
"Every new lead should get a welcome text"Workflow
"Tell our whole list about Friday's sale"Campaign
"Remind people 24 hours before their appointment"Workflow
"Send this month's newsletter to all subscribers"Campaign
💡
The one-line test: if it depends on when something happens to a specific contact, it's a Workflow. If it depends on a calendar date for a whole list, it's a Campaign. A client asking for "automation" sometimes really means a one-time blast. Ask which one before you build.

APIs & Webhooks

These two words scare a lot of VAs. They shouldn't. You don't need to be a programmer. You need to understand what they do so you can talk about them, set them up from instructions, and know when to reach for them.

What's an API? (The Restaurant Analogy)

API = the waiter

You (one app) sit at a table. The kitchen (another app) has what you want. You don't barge into the kitchen. You tell the waiter (the API) your order, and the waiter brings back exactly what you asked for. An API (Application Programming Interface) is just the messenger that lets two apps talk to each other in a structured, agreed-upon way.

When someone says "GHL has an API," they mean: other tools can ask GHL for data (like a contact) or tell GHL to do something (like create a contact) automatically, without a human clicking buttons.

Webhook vs. API: The Key Distinction

API call: you ASK (pull)
Your tool reaches out and requests something: "Hey GHL, give me this contact's details." You initiate it. Like phoning the kitchen to ask if the food is ready.
Webhook: it TELLS you (push)
GHL automatically pushes data the moment something happens: "A new lead just came in. Here are their details." You don't ask; it notifies you. Like the kitchen ringing a bell when the food's up.

Simple rule: A webhook is an action inside a GHL workflow that sends data out to another tool the instant a trigger fires. It's the most common way you'll connect GHL to the outside world, and you set it up by pasting in a URL, not by coding.

Security: API keys are passwords

An API key gives full programmatic access to a client's account. Treat it like a password: never paste it into a public chat, never email it in plain text, never store it where others can see it. If you ever expose one by accident, tell the client immediately so it can be regenerated. This is non-negotiable professional hygiene.

The Rule

API keys are credentials, not code. Store them in a password manager or your client's secure settings. Never in a chat message, a doc, or a spreadsheet that others can access.

Three Ways to Connect GHL to Other Tools

1
Native integrations

Built right into GHL. Connect with a few clicks in Settings. Examples: Google/Outlook calendar sync, Stripe payments, Facebook & Instagram, Google My Business, Mailgun. Always check for a native integration first; it's the most reliable.

2
No-code connectors (Zapier & Make)

When there's no native option, Zapier or Make bridge GHL to thousands of apps using triggers and webhooks. Example: "When a deal is Won in GHL → create a project in ClickUp → post a message in Slack." This is the VA's everyday integration toolkit (from Day 5).

3
Direct API / webhook

For custom or developer-built connections, tools talk to GHL directly through its API. You generally won't build these, but you'll recognise them and can follow a guide to plug things in.

4
Marketplace apps

Third-party tools built specifically for GHL (call tracking, proposal software, review widgets) listed in the GHL App Marketplace. They install with an API key or one-click auth, more setup than a native toggle, but no middleman tool required.

Day 7 · Session 8

When an Integration Stops Working

Integrations break quietly, not loudly. A Zapier connection can sit "on" for weeks after a step inside it silently fails. Knowing how to triage saves you from guessing.

1
Confirm the trigger actually fired

In GHL, check the workflow's enrollment history. If the contact never entered the workflow, the problem is upstream of the integration entirely.

2
Check the webhook log

Most workflow steps show whether the webhook fired and what data it sent. If it fired but the other tool didn't react, the problem is on the receiving end.

3
Open the Zapier/Make run history

Both tools log every run, including failed ones, with the exact error. This is usually where the real answer is.

4
Re-test with a fresh test contact

Reproduce it live rather than guessing. If it works for the test contact, ask what's different about the original one (missing field, bad phone format, etc.).

The most common culprit

A field GHL sends as blank or differently formatted than the other tool expects, e.g. a phone number missing a country code. Most "broken integration" tickets turn out to be a data-format mismatch, not a connection failure.

Funnels & Forms, Where Triggers Start

Every recipe today started with a trigger. Forms and funnels are where most of those triggers are actually born, so a quick look at both closes the loop.

1
Forms & surveys

A form is a single page of fields (name, email, "what service do you need?"). A survey is a multi-step version of the same idea. Both live inside a funnel or website page, and submitting one is one of the most common workflow triggers there is.

2
Funnels

A funnel is a small sequence of pages built to move a visitor toward one action, e.g. a landing page → a booking page → a thank-you page. GHL's funnel builder is drag-and-drop, no coding required.

The Chain You Now Understand End to End

Funnel
Visitor lands on a page
Form
Visitor submits their info
Trigger
Form submitted fires the workflow
Action
Recipe 1 takes over from here

This is the full picture: a funnel brings the visitor in, a form captures them, a trigger hands them to a workflow, and the recipes you learned today take it from there.

Day 7 · Session 9

Your Role as a VA

You're rarely designing a funnel from scratch. You're far more often editing one, adding a field to a form, or troubleshooting why a submission didn't trigger anything.

TaskWhat You're Actually Doing
"Add a phone field to the form"Open the form editor, drag in a phone field, mark it required, save
"The funnel isn't capturing leads"Check the form's submit action is linked to a workflow trigger, not just saving silently
"Swap this image on the landing page"Open the funnel's page editor, replace the element, republish
"Duplicate this funnel for a new offer"Clone it, update copy and the linked workflow trigger, publish under a new URL
The connection that matters

A funnel without a form can't trigger anything. A form without a linked workflow just sits in a contact record doing nothing. Always trace the full chain: page → form → trigger → workflow.
