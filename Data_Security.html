Security & Privacy · Reference

Data Security, Handling & Privacy

A complete reference for how to handle client data, what can go into AI tools, how regulations apply by industry, and what to do when something goes wrong.

What this covers
  • The Golden Rule — why AI tools are not private environments
  • Data Classification — the 4 levels and what each one allows
  • The AI Boundary — exactly what can and cannot go into AI tools, plus how to anonymise
  • Key Terms — PII, PHI, PCI-DSS, GDPR defined simply
  • Regulations by Industry — HIPAA, legal privilege, finance, email/SMS
  • Passwords & API Keys — credential security standards
  • File Sharing & Storage — Drive, email, Slack rules
  • Verification — the 7-point checklist before anything leaves your hands
  • Phishing & Social Engineering — red flags and how to respond
  • Breach Protocol — the 5 steps if something goes wrong
This is not optional reading

Data security is part of your job, not a bonus skill. As a VA with access to client accounts, CRMs, and automations, you handle sensitive data every day. A single mistake — a wrong recipient, a pasted API key, PHI in a prompt — can have real consequences for the client and for KeyPlayers. This reference exists so you never have to guess.

Security & Privacy · Section 1

The Golden Rule

The single most important principle before you open any AI tool.

AI tools are not private

When you type something into Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, or any public AI tool, that information leaves your device and is processed on external servers. Most major AI providers state they may use inputs to improve their models. Even if they don't, the information has left your control.

Treat every AI tool like a public space. If you wouldn't say it out loud in a coffee shop in front of strangers, don't type it into an AI tool.

You are the decision layer

Every piece of AI output is a draft. It is your job to review, correct, and approve before anything goes to a client, lead, or the public. Catching fluff, errors, and hallucinations is not optional — it is the core part of your role. AI thinks fast. You think right.

When in doubt, don't

If you are uncertain whether something is safe to include in a prompt, stop and ask your team lead or client before proceeding. It is always better to pause for 5 minutes than to expose information that cannot be taken back.

Security & Privacy · Section 2

Data Classification Levels

Before using any information in a prompt or sharing it anywhere, classify it. The level tells you what you can and cannot do with it.

LevelWhat it meansExamplesCan it go in AI?
Public Already publicly available. No restrictions. Website copy, published pricing, public social posts Yes
Internal Not public but not sensitive. General operations. Meeting agendas, team processes, generic SOPs, tone guides Yes, with care
Confidential Sensitive business or client data. Limited access. Unreleased campaigns, pricing strategy, client names, employee info Anonymise first
Restricted Legally protected or highly sensitive. Zero tolerance. PHI, PII, financial records, legal case files, API keys, passwords Never
How to apply this

Before writing a prompt, classify every piece of information you plan to include. If anything is Confidential, anonymise it first. If anything is Restricted, remove it entirely and substitute a placeholder. The AI doesn't need real details to help you — it needs the structure of the problem.

Security & Privacy · Section 3

The AI Boundary

Apply this every time you build a prompt. No exceptions.

Never Put These In
  • Full names and contact details of real clients or leads
  • Financial account numbers, balances, or transactions
  • Medical information of any kind
  • Legal case specifics or privileged communications
  • Passwords, API keys, or authentication tokens
  • Social security numbers, tax IDs, passport numbers
  • Confidential business strategy or unreleased products
  • Employee salaries, HR records, or performance data
  • Credit card numbers or payment information
  • Any data your client has marked confidential
Safe to Use
  • General business context — industry, service type, tone
  • Anonymised examples with placeholders ("Client A", "a homeowner")
  • Template structures, frameworks, and SOP drafts
  • Published public information about a business
  • Your own writing for review or improvement
  • Marketing copy, campaign ideas, content briefs
  • Internal meeting notes with no PHI or PII
  • Generic workflow logic and process documentation
  • Fictional or hypothetical examples
  • Publicly available product descriptions or FAQ content

Anonymising Before You Prompt

You can still use AI for sensitive tasks — you just need to strip or replace identifying details before typing. This takes less than 30 seconds and eliminates most of the risk.

Before — don't do this

"Write a follow-up email for John Smith at Sunrise Dental in Austin, TX who called about an unpaid $47,500 invoice from March 2026."

After — safe version

"Write a follow-up email for a dental clinic client who called about an overdue invoice. Professional and friendly tone. Include an offer to discuss payment options."

The replacement rule
  • Replace names with roles: "John Smith" → "a client", "Dr. Patel" → "a healthcare provider"
  • Replace specific amounts with ranges: "$47,500" → "a large invoice"
  • Replace exact dates with relative times: "March 14, 2026" → "last month"
  • Replace company names with industries: "Sunrise Dental" → "a dental clinic"
  • Replace locations with regions: "Austin, TX" → "a Texas client"
Security & Privacy · Section 4

Key Terms

Definitions every VA must know before working with client data.

PII — Personally Identifiable Information

Any information that can identify a specific person, alone or in combination with other data. Full name, email address, phone number, home or work address, date of birth, IP address, SSN, passport number, driver's license, biometric data, or device identifiers. Even a combination of harmless-looking details — job title + city + employer — can constitute PII.

  • Never input PII into public AI tools or unauthorised systems
  • Use placeholders in prompts: "a 45-year-old client in Texas" — not their real details
  • Store files containing PII in approved, access-controlled locations only
PHI — Protected Health Information (HIPAA)

Any health information linked or potentially linked to an identifiable individual. Covered under HIPAA in the United States. Includes: diagnoses, treatment plans, prescriptions, test results, appointment times, insurance information, billing details, mental health records, and clinical notes.

  • PHI must never be entered into AI tools — not even with names removed if the condition + date + location could still identify someone
  • AI note-takers (Fathom, Granola) must never be used on patient calls or clinical meetings
  • Violations can result in fines of $100 to $50,000 per violation. The client and the agency can both be held liable

Key Terms (continued)

PCI-DSS — Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard

A set of security standards governing how payment card information is stored, processed, and transmitted. As a VA, you will likely never process payments directly — but you may encounter card data in CRM notes, email threads, or spreadsheets.

  • Never type, copy, or forward credit card numbers — not in Slack, email, AI tools, or documents
  • If you find card data in a client's CRM or files, notify your team lead immediately. Do not move or copy it
  • Card numbers should never be stored in plain text by anyone
GDPR — General Data Protection Regulation

EU regulation governing the collection, storage, and use of personal data for EU residents. Applies to any business that serves EU customers, regardless of where the business is based. Key principles: data collected with consent, used only for stated purpose, stored only as long as needed, and individuals have the right to request deletion.

  • If your client has EU-based contacts in their CRM, GDPR applies to how that data is handled
  • Do not add EU contacts to campaigns without confirmed consent on file
  • If a contact requests deletion of their data, escalate to your client or team lead immediately
Security & Privacy · Section 5

Regulations by Industry

Different client industries are governed by different rules. Know the rules before you start working in a niche. Ignorance is not a defence.

HIPAA
Healthcare

Protects patient health information. Applies to clinics, hospitals, therapists, dentists, and any business that handles health records.

Never input patient data into AI. No PHI in prompts, spreadsheets, or Slack. AI note-takers for staff meetings only — never patient calls.
ABA / Privilege
Legal

Attorney-client privilege protects confidential communications. Sharing privileged information can waive that protection permanently and expose the firm to liability.

No case-specific details, client names, or legal strategy in AI tools. Templates only — never case content or actual communications.
SEC / FINRA
Finance & Investments

Financial advisors and brokers operate under strict rules on communication, record-keeping, and advice. Client financial data is highly regulated and subject to compliance review at any time.

No account numbers, portfolio data, SSNs, or investment details in AI tools. All communications may be audited.
CAN-SPAM / GDPR
Email & SMS (All Industries)

Any business sending marketing emails or SMS must comply with consent and opt-out laws. Applies globally — not just in the EU or US. Violations can result in significant fines.

Never send to contacts without confirmed consent. Always include unsubscribe options. Honour opt-outs within 10 business days.
Contractors & Home Service

Lower regulatory exposure than healthcare or legal, but still requires care. Verify all pricing language before sending, never share a client's home address beyond those who need it for the job, and confirm any financing or warranty terms are accurate before automating them into messages.

Security & Privacy · Section 6

Passwords & API Keys

API keys are passwords. A leaked API key gives someone full access to whatever service it belongs to — billing, data, automations, everything. This has caused businesses to lose thousands overnight.

Never Do These
  • Paste an API key or password into an AI chat tool
  • Share credentials in Slack, WhatsApp, or email
  • Store passwords in a shared Google Doc or spreadsheet
  • Use the same password across multiple platforms
  • Screenshot a credentials page and send it to someone
  • Write API keys in plain text in any code or automation you build
  • Leave credentials in a chat thread after the task is done
Always Do These
  • Use a password manager (1Password, Bitwarden, LastPass) for all credentials
  • Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) on every platform you access
  • Store API keys in the secrets manager or environment variables of your automation tool
  • Use a separate API key per integration so a single leak doesn't compromise everything
  • Rotate a key immediately if you suspect it was exposed
  • When a project ends, request that all shared credentials are changed
Day 7 reminder

You covered this in Day 7: API keys = passwords. This principle applies everywhere, not just GHL. Every service you connect in Make.com, Zapier, or any automation has an API key. If a key is stored somewhere anyone with the link can read it, it's already compromised — even if no one has used it yet.

Security & Privacy · Section 7

File Sharing & Storage

Where data lives matters as much as what the data is. A confidential file in the wrong folder is already exposed.

Google Drive

Always verify sharing settings before uploading sensitive files. "Anyone with the link" means anyone on the internet — including search engines that index public Drive content. Use "Restricted" for confidential files and share only with named individuals. Never put credentials, PHI, or PII in a shared drive that isn't strictly access-controlled.

Email Attachments

Verify the recipient's email address before sending any file containing client data. One wrong character — one transposed letter — can send confidential information to a stranger. If a client sends you sensitive files by email, do not forward them or store them outside the agreed system.

Slack & Messaging Apps

Slack messages are logged and searchable by workspace admins. Do not share passwords, client PII, or sensitive files in Slack channels. Use direct messages only for sensitive one-time communication, never for storing anything long-term.

The clean-up rule

When a project ends or a client offboards, remove their data from all personal storage locations — Downloads, Desktop, local folders. Sensitive files should only exist in the approved client-specific location, not scattered across your personal device.

Security & Privacy · Section 8

The Verification Habit

AI tools are confident by design. They state incorrect facts, invent names, hallucinate statistics, and produce content that sounds authoritative but is wrong. Your review is the last line of defence before a client sees it.

7-Point Checklist — Before Any Output Leaves Your Hands
  • Correct names and details? Re-read every proper noun. The AI may have used the wrong client name or brand.
  • No hallucinated facts or statistics? Any number, date, or claim not in your prompt needs verification. AI invents plausible-sounding data.
  • No pricing or scope errors? AI sometimes invents numbers. Never send a price without confirming it with the client first.
  • Tone matches the brand? Does it sound like the client, or does it sound like a generic AI template?
  • No overconfident language? Phrases like "studies show" or "research confirms" without a source are hallucination red flags.
  • Not outdated? AI knowledge has a cutoff date. Time-sensitive content must be checked against current sources.
  • No legal, medical, or financial claims? Never let AI-generated legal, medical, or financial advice reach a client without professional review.
The edit is the job

Checking AI output is not a bonus step — it is the primary skill that separates a VA who delivers trusted work from one who forwards AI drafts unchanged. The client pays for judgment, not throughput. Your review is what makes the AI useful. Without it, the output is a liability.

Security & Privacy · Section 9

Phishing & Social Engineering

The most common security threat is a convincing message. As a VA with access to client accounts, CRMs, and automations, you are a high-value target.

Red flags in emails and messages
  • Urgency: "Your account will be suspended in 24 hours" or "Act immediately"
  • Login requests: "Click here to verify your account" or "Re-enter your credentials"
  • Suspicious sender: the email domain doesn't match the company (e.g. support@g00gle.com)
  • Unexpected security alerts: password reset emails or login verification requests you didn't initiate
  • Unusual payment requests: a "client" asking you to purchase gift cards, wire money, or change payment details
  • Credential requests via SMS or WhatsApp: no legitimate platform asks for your password through a chat app
What to do if you're unsure
  • Do not click links in suspicious emails — go directly to the website by typing the URL manually in a new tab
  • Verify any unusual request by calling the person on a known number — not one provided in the suspicious message
  • Report suspicious emails to your team lead before taking any action
  • If you clicked something suspicious, change your passwords immediately and notify your team lead

If Something Goes Wrong

Mistakes happen. What matters is how fast and honestly they're handled. Trying to cover up a data incident makes it significantly worse — legally and operationally.

Stop and report immediately

If you believe client data has been exposed or a breach has occurred, stop what you're doing and report it. Do not try to fix it yourself first. Every hour of delay increases the risk and the damage. Your job is to report, not resolve it alone.

  1. 1
    Document what happened. Write down what you did, what was exposed, when it happened, and what systems were involved. Do this immediately while details are fresh. A clear incident record is critical.
  2. 2
    Notify your team lead now. Do not wait until the end of the day. Call or message right away regardless of severity. They need to assess the situation and may need to notify the client promptly.
  3. 3
    Contain the exposure if possible. If a file was shared with the wrong person, revoke access. If a credential was exposed, rotate it. If an automation sent to the wrong contact, stop the workflow now.
  4. 4
    Do not delete evidence. Do not delete emails, messages, or files related to the incident. These may be needed for investigation. Preserve everything as-is until your team lead advises otherwise.
  5. 5
    Cooperate fully and learn from it. Support the response. Once resolved, review what happened and identify how to prevent it in the future. A well-handled incident is a learning moment. A covered-up one is a liability.
