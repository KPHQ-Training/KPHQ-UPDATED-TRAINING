Automation Foundations

Days 1 to 3 were about using AI yourself. Now we shift from doing the work to designing systems that do it for you. Automation is simply work that runs without a human pressing the buttons.

Where we are

You can now prompt well, pick the right tool, and use Claude's features. Automation is the next leap: instead of doing a task each time, you set it up once and it repeats on its own.

Doing it vs designing it

A VA who does tasks trades time for output: every result costs minutes. A VA who designs systems builds something that keeps producing after they step away. Same person, very different leverage.

Manual: you do it
You repeat the same steps by hand every time. It works, but it only happens when you are at your desk, and it costs you minutes on every single run.
Automated: it runs
You set the steps up once. After that it fires on its own, instantly, every time, whether you are working, asleep, or on a call.
One quick example

You send the same welcome email to every new client. By hand that is five minutes each, every time. As an automation, you build it once and every new client gets it in seconds, forever.

The Principle

Automation does not replace you. It removes the repetitive part so your time goes to judgment and relationships, the work only a human can do.

Four types of automation

Not all automations work the same way. Knowing the type helps you pick the right tool and set the right expectations with your client.

TypeHow it worksVA example
ScheduledRuns at a set time, like an alarm clockWeekly report every Monday at 8am
TriggeredFires the moment something happensNew form submitted → welcome email sent
Multi-stepOne trigger sets off a chain of actionsNew lead → CRM → email → Slack ping
AI-poweredAI reads, writes, or decides as part of the stepsNew email → Claude drafts reply → you approve
Most VA automations are triggered or multi-step

Scheduled automations handle reports and recurring tasks. Triggered multi-step automations handle the bulk of daily VA work: lead intake, onboarding, follow-ups, and notifications.

Day 4 · Session 2

Trigger and Action

Every automation, no matter how advanced, is the same simple shape: when something happens, do something. Learn this one pattern and you can read or design any automation you meet.

The full shape has four parts. A trigger starts it, an optional filter keeps it precise, one or more actions do the work, and you get a result.

Trigger
when X happens
Filter
only if it matters
Action
do Y, then Z
Result
done, no hands
Read it as a sentence

When a new client form is submitted, if they chose a paid plan, then add them to the CRM and send the welcome email. Trigger, filter, actions, result.

Common triggers and actions

You will see the same handful of triggers and actions over and over. Once they are familiar, most automations are just new combinations of these.

Triggers (when this happens)Actions (do this)
A form is submittedSend an email or a message
A new email arrivesCreate or update a record (CRM, sheet)
A scheduled time (daily, weekly)Add a row to a spreadsheet
A new row or record is addedNotify a person in Slack or text
A message is receivedGenerate a draft or summary with AI
Filters keep it precise

A filter is a simple "only if" rule that stops the automation from firing when it should not: only if the email is from a client, only if the order is over a set amount, only on weekdays. Filters are how you avoid noisy, wrong, or duplicate runs.

Day 4 · Session 3

AI Automation

Regular automation moves data from A to B. AI automation adds a thinking step in the middle. That one difference opens up a completely different category of task you can hand off.

Regular automation
Follows fixed rules. It can copy, move, and send — but it cannot understand what something means, vary a message for context, or make any kind of judgment call.
AI automation
Adds a thinking step. Claude reads the input, decides what to do or say based on your prompt, and passes the result to the next action. It handles variability.
Think of it this way

Regular automation is like a mail sorter: it moves things based on fixed labels. AI automation is like a smart assistant: it reads the message, understands it, and decides what comes next.

How Claude fits into a workflow

You do not need to code to add Claude to an automation. Zapier, Make, and n8n all have a built-in Claude step you can drop in anywhere.

New email
trigger: Gmail

drafts a reply
You approve

done

Claude is the middle step. It receives text, processes it with a prompt you write, and passes the result forward. Your prompt is the instruction: "Summarize this in 3 bullets", "Write a short reply", "Classify this as urgent, normal, or low-priority."

Data note

Any text you pass to Claude through an automation goes to Anthropic's API. Do not send private client data without checking the client's data policy first.

Day 4 · Session 4

What to Automate (and What Not To)

The skill is not automating everything. It is knowing what to hand to a machine and what to keep human. That judgment is exactly what makes an operator valuable, and hard to replace.

Good to automate when it is...Keep it human when it is...
Repetitive - the same steps each timeJudgment-heavy - needs a call only you can make
Rule-based - clear if-this-then-that logicRelationship-led - a real person should reply
High-volume - happens often enough to matterSensitive - money, contracts, complaints, health
Predictable - few surprises in the inputRare or one-off - not worth the build time
The Principle

Automate the boring and repeatable. Protect the human and high-stakes. The best operators automate the grunt work to free up their judgment, not replace it.

Keep a human in the loop

Automating a step does not mean trusting it blindly. For anything risky or sensitive, add an approval step: the automation prepares the work, then a person reviews and approves before it sends, posts, or pays.

A safe pattern

Let the automation do 90 percent (gather, draft, organize) and keep the last 10 percent human (review, approve, send). You get the speed of automation with the safety of a human check.

Carry-over from Day 1

Regulated or client-private data flows only through approved, contracted tools, never a random free connector. The data-safety question comes first, every time.

Guardrails: rules that keep everyone safe

Automation that breaks quietly is worse than no automation. These eight rules protect your client, their contacts, and your reputation as a VA.

#RuleWhy it matters
1Test-send first — always send to yourself before any real audienceOne wrong batch email is very hard to undo
2No sensitive data through free tools — names, emails, and payment info stay in approved platformsFree tools may share or store data in ways your client has not consented to
3Money, contracts, complaints = human approval — automation prepares; a person approvesHigh-stakes actions need a human checkpoint, no exceptions
4Set up error alerts — know the moment something breaksSilent failures do damage before you notice them
5Know your off switch — be able to pause the automation in under one minuteIf it misfires, you need to stop it immediately
6Tell the client before you deploy — no surprise automations touching their contacts or dataTrust requires transparency, especially early on
7Check the run history — most platforms log every execution; review it weeklyCatches edge cases and errors before they pile up
8Watch the first 5 runs manually — do not fully step away until you have seen it work on real dataTest data never catches everything; real conditions always surface new issues
The short version

If something goes wrong, you are the one who has to explain it. Build as if you will have to defend every decision — because sometimes you will.

Day 4 · Compliance

Compliance: Know the Rules Before You Automate

Compliance is not bureaucracy. It is the legal and policy framework that protects your client's customers — and protects your client's business from liability. As a VA, you follow these rules even if you did not write them.

FrameworkWho it coversWhat it means for your automations
GDPRAnyone handling data of EU citizensPersonal data must be collected with consent, stored lawfully, and deletable on request — check every tool in the flow
CCPAData of California residentsConsumers can opt out of data use; any automation touching California contacts must honour that flag
HIPAAUS healthcare dataHealth information cannot flow through any non-HIPAA-compliant tool under any circumstances — full stop
SOC 2Business / enterprise SaaS dataAsk if the connector tools you are using are SOC 2 certified before routing client business data through them
The one question every VA must ask

"Does my client have a data policy that covers this automation?" If the answer is unclear — stop. Ask before you build. A five-minute conversation now prevents a legal incident later.

Your responsibility

Ignorance is not a defence. If you automate data into a non-compliant tool, liability can fall on the VA and the client. When in doubt, escalate — never guess.

Data security in your automations

Compliance tells you what rules to follow. Security tells you how to protect the data while the automation is running. You need both — one without the other still leaves you exposed.

Data typeLevelRule
Task updates, calendar blocks, content drafts, internal notesGREEN — generally safeProceed, but still verify the tool is approved by the client
Names, emails, phone numbers, business contact infoYELLOW — check firstConfirm the client's privacy policy covers this data flowing through these tools
Health records, financial data, passwords, PII of minors, legal documentsRED — stopDo not automate without explicit written approval from the client or their legal team
Four security rules — no exceptions
  • Never store credentials in the workflow text — use the platform's built-in secrets manager
  • Request only the permissions the automation actually needs — not read/write/delete everything
  • Audit tool access every 90 days — remove connections to tools you no longer use
  • Rotate API keys immediately if a tool is compromised, deprecated, or shared by accident

Day 4 · Session 6

Plan before you build

Rushing into an automation tool before thinking it through is how you end up with something that half-works, breaks silently, and takes twice as long to fix. Five minutes of planning prevents five hours of debugging.

Rush: build first, think later
  • Copy a template and fill in the fields
  • Test it live on real contacts
  • Debug from error messages
  • Realise halfway through it breaks client policy
Plan: think first, build once
  • Sketch the flow on paper or in a doc first
  • Break it into discrete tasks and check each tool
  • Test on dummy data before going near real clients
  • Deliver something that works and passes compliance
QuestionWhy it matters
What exact problem does this solve?Prevents building automations that are clever but useless
What triggers it, and can that trigger misfire?Misfire = accidental sends or duplicate data
What data does it touch, and is that data sensitive?Compliance and security check before a single tool is opened
What should happen if it fails?Every automation needs a fallback or an alert — not a silent failure
Who needs to approve this before it goes live?Client sign-off protects you from building the wrong thing
Break it into tasks

List every step the automation takes as a separate task before you open the tool. Automation is just tasks in order — and tasks you have not written down are tasks you will forget to test.

Day 4 · Session 6

Map It First

Every automation is made of the same four building blocks. Know each one by name and you can read, build, or fix any workflow you encounter.

Trigger
The event that starts everything. Nothing runs until the trigger fires — a form submitted, a time reached, a message received.
Condition (if/else)
A yes/no question that splits the flow into two paths. Each answer runs different actions. This is how automations handle real-world variety.
Action
Something the automation actually does: send a message, create a record, update a field, generate text with AI, or notify a person.
Result
The finished output after all steps complete. This is what you promised the client when you said "I will automate this."

A four-question map

You do not need special software to map a workflow. Answer these four questions and you have a buildable plan.

AskYou are finding the...
1. What starts it?Trigger (the event that kicks it off)
2. What are the steps, in order?Actions (each thing that should happen)
3. Are there any "only if" decisions?Filters (conditions that keep it precise)
4. What is the finished result, and who needs to know?Output (and the notification)
The Principle

A workflow you can describe in one clear sentence is a workflow you can automate. If you cannot describe it cleanly, you are not ready to build it yet.

From map to live automation

Once you have your four-question map, building is just following a checklist. Do not skip steps — especially the test step.

  1. Map it first — trigger, steps in order, filters, and the result
  2. Pick your tool — match to what the client already uses; free tier if there is no preference
  3. Build the trigger alone and test it — make sure it fires before you add any actions
  4. Add one action at a time — test each step before adding the next one
  5. Run it on test data — never test on real clients or live records
  6. Review the full output — does the final result look exactly right?
  7. Go live and watch — monitor the first 3 to 5 real runs before you step away
The golden rule

Build one step at a time. Test one step at a time. The most common mistake is building five steps and then wondering which one broke. Go slowly so you can go confidently.

QA checklist before you go live

Run through this before any automation touches real clients. It takes under two minutes and prevents a lot of avoidable problems.

CheckWhat to look for
Trigger fires correctlyDid your test event actually start the automation?
Filter catches the right recordsDid it run when it should — and skip when it should not?
Every action completedDid each step finish without an error message?
Output is accurateIs the email, message, or record exactly what was intended?
Right destinationDid it go to the right person, channel, or folder?
No duplicatesDid it run once — not two or three times?
Error alert set upIf it breaks, who gets notified and how?
Document it after QA

Write a one-paragraph note: what the automation does, what starts it, and who owns it. Future you — or a colleague covering for you — will need to edit it months from now and the notes will save hours.

Best practices as a VA who automates

Technical skill gets you to a working automation. These habits get you to a trustworthy, sustainable one — the kind that makes clients want to keep you long-term.

PracticeWhat it looks like in action
Understand the manual process firstIf you cannot walk through it step by step by hand, you are not ready to automate it
Build simple, then addGet one trigger and one action working before adding filters, branches, or AI steps
Name everything clearly"Welcome Email — New Paid Leads — Zapier" is infinitely better than "Zap 47"
One automation, one jobWhen a flow gets complex, split it. Smaller automations are easier to fix and easier to explain
Document as you buildTrigger, each step, owner, test data location, and the date you last reviewed it
Review monthlyAPIs change, tools update, business rules evolve — check that everything still works
Never be the only one who knowsShare your documentation so a colleague can take over if you are unavailable
Track and share the winsRecord time saved and show it to your client — this is what builds your value and justifies your rate
The Principle

The automation is not the deliverable. The consistent, reliable outcome it produces is. Anyone can build a flow that works once. Build one that works every time.
