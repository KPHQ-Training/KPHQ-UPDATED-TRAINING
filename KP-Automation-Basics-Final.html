Session 1: How Automations Work

What is an automation?

An automation is a task your tools do for you — no clicking, no copy-pasting. You set it up once, and it runs on its own every time the trigger happens.

Think of it like a rule you set in your email inbox: "If an email is from my boss, mark it as important." You wrote that rule once. Now your inbox handles it forever.

What is an if/else condition?

An if/else condition is the decision point inside your automation. It tells the tool: "Check this one thing — and depending on the answer, take this path or that path."

Real Example

A Google Form comes in. Your automation checks the "Urgency" field.

  • If it says "High" → email the manager immediately
  • Else (anything other than High) → log it to a spreadsheet

Session 2: Gmail Label Router

Make Gmail — When an email arrives, check what it is and label it automatically.

This automation reads your incoming emails and sorts them into labels — no manual inbox management needed.

What This Automation Does
  • If the subject contains "invoice" → apply the label "Invoices" and star the email
  • Else if the sender is from your client's domain → apply the label "Clients"
  • Else → do nothing (the email stays as normal)

Part 1 — Account Setup (do once)

  1. Go to make.com in your browser and click Sign up for freeUse your Google account to sign up — it's faster and you won't need a separate password.
  2. Once you're in, click Create a new scenario (top right button)
  3. You'll see a blank canvas with a large + in the middle — click it

Part 2 — Set Up the Trigger (Gmail)

  1. In the search box that appears, type Gmail and select it from the list
  2. Choose Watch Emails as your trigger — this means Make will check for new emails on a schedule
  3. Click Add next to the Google connection field, then click Continue and authorize your Google accountA Google login popup will appear. Select the same account you use for work and click Allow.
  4. Set Folder to Inbox
  5. Set Maximum number of results to 5 (this controls how many emails it processes per run)
  6. Click OK

Part 3 — Add the Router (the If/Else)

  1. Hover over the Gmail circle on the canvas and click the small + that appears to its right
  2. Search for Router and select it — a Router splits one path into two (or more) separate branches
  3. You'll now see two empty route boxes coming out of the Router — these are your two conditions

Part 4 — Route 1: Invoice Emails

  1. Click the wrench icon (🔧) on Route 1 to set its filter condition
  2. Under Label, type: Route 1 — Invoices
  3. Click Add AND rule and set: Subject | Contains (case insensitive) | type invoiceLowercase is fine — the "case insensitive" option means it will also match "Invoice" or "INVOICE".
  4. Click OK
  5. Now click the + at the end of Route 1 to add an action. Search Gmail → choose Add a Label to an Email
  6. Under Message ID, click in the field — a panel will open on the left. Click the Gmail icon and select Message ID from the dropdownThis maps the email that triggered the automation to this action.
  7. Under Label Name, type: Invoices (Make will create this label in Gmail if it doesn't exist yet)
  8. Click OK
  9. Click the + after that label action. Search Gmail → choose Modify an Email
  10. Map Message ID the same way as before. Then find Starred and set it to Yes
  11. Click OK

Part 5 — Route 2: Client Emails

  1. Click the wrench icon on Route 2
  2. Label it: Route 2 — Clients
  3. Add rule: From | Contains | type your client's email domain — example: acmecorp.comYou only need the domain (everything after the @). This catches any email from anyone at that company.
  4. Click OK
  5. Add a Gmail → Add a Label action on Route 2. Map the Message ID and set the label name to Clients
  6. Click OK

Part 6 — Test and Turn On

  1. Click Run once (bottom left of the screen) — Make will grab your last 5 emails and run them through both routesYou'll see green checkmarks on each step that ran successfully, and the number of items processed.
  2. Open your Gmail and check that the matching emails now have the correct labels applied
  3. If everything looks right, click the toggle switch at the bottom of the screen to turn the scenario ONOnce ON, Make will check for new emails every 15 minutes automatically (on the free plan).
You Only Authorize Google Once

Once you connect your Google account in Make, it stays connected for all your future scenarios. You won't need to log in again unless you manually disconnect it.

⚡ Practice

Look at your last 10 emails and decide what two labels would be most useful for your inbox. Build this scenario using your own label names instead of "Invoices" and "Clients." What trigger word or sender would you filter by?

Session 3: Google Form → Conditional Email

Make Google Forms Gmail Google Sheets — When a form is submitted, check the urgency and respond differently depending on the answer.

This is the most common automation shape in VA work. Learn this pattern and you'll recognize it everywhere.

What This Automation Does
  • If the "Urgency Level" answer = "High" → send an email alert to the manager right away
  • Else (Normal or anything else) → add the submission to a Google Sheet log only

Part 1 — Build the Google Form

  1. Go to forms.google.com and click the + to create a new blank form
  2. Title the form: Task Request Form
  3. Click on the first question field. Type: Your Name — leave the type as Short answer
  4. Click Add question (the + icon on the right sidebar). Type: Urgency Level
  5. Click the question type dropdown (currently says "Multiple choice") and change it to Dropdown
  6. Add two options: type High in Option 1, and Normal in Option 2
  7. Click the eye icon (Preview) at the top right to see your form, then submit two test responses:
    — First response: Name = Test High, Urgency = High
    — Second response: Name = Test Normal, Urgency = NormalYou need real test responses before Make can detect the form. This step is important — don't skip it.

Part 2 — Create a Google Sheet for Logging

  1. Go to sheets.google.com and create a new blank spreadsheet
  2. Name it: Task Request Log
  3. In Row 1, add these column headers: Name | Urgency | Date SubmittedLeave this tab open — you'll need to select it inside Make.

Part 3 — Set Up Make

  1. In Make, create a new scenario
  2. Click the + on the canvas, search for Google Forms, and select Watch Responses
  3. Connect your Google account if prompted
  4. Under Spreadsheet, find and select your form — it will appear as a spreadsheet file in your Google DriveGoogle Forms stores responses in a linked spreadsheet. Make accesses it this way.
  5. Set Maximum number of results to 5, then click OK

Part 4 — Add the Router

  1. Click the + after Google Forms and add a Router

Part 5 — Route 1: High Urgency → Email the Manager

  1. Click the wrench icon on Route 1. Label it: Route 1 — High
  2. Add a rule: Urgency Level | Equal to | HighThe field name comes from your form question. Click in the first dropdown to see the list of form fields.
  3. Click OK
  4. Add action: Gmail → Send an Email
  5. Fill in the fields:
    • To: type your manager's email address
    • Subject: click in the field, then click the Google Forms icon on the left panel and select Your Name — then type around it so the subject reads: URGENT Task Request from {{Your Name}}
    • Content: type a short message, e.g. A high-urgency task was just submitted. Please review the form immediately.
  6. Click OK

Part 6 — Route 2: Normal → Log to Sheet

  1. Click the wrench icon on Route 2. Label it: Route 2 — Normal
  2. Leave this route with no filter — it will catch everything that did not match Route 1In Make, a route with no filter acts as the "else" — it catches everything that didn't match an earlier route.
  3. Click OK
  4. Add action: Google Sheets → Add a Row
  5. Select your Task Request Log spreadsheet and the correct sheet tab
  6. Map the columns:
    • Name → click the field, select Your Name from the form data
    • Urgency → select Urgency Level from the form data
    • Date Submitted → type {{now}} — this inserts the current date/time automatically
  7. Click OK

Part 7 — Test and Turn On

  1. Click Run once — Make will process your two test responses
  2. Check your Gmail inbox — you should have received the URGENT email for the "High" test response
  3. Check your Task Request Log spreadsheet — the "Normal" test response should appear as a new row
  4. If both worked, click the toggle to turn the scenario ON
This is the Core Pattern

Trigger → check a value → two different outcomes. Once you understand this shape, you can apply it to almost any repetitive decision in your workflow. The form and the labels will change — the logic stays the same.

⚡ Practice

Think of a form you currently fill out or receive in your work. What one question could you add an if/else rule to? Write out: "If the answer is ____, then ____. Otherwise, ____." You don't need to build it — just write the logic.

Session 4: Google Sheet → Task Creator

ActivePieces Google Sheets Google Tasks Gmail — When a new row is added to your lead tracker, check the status and either create a task or send a review email.

This automation uses ActivePieces — a free tool with no credit card required. It's a great starting point if Make feels like too much at once.

What This Automation Does
  • If the Status column = "Qualified" → create a Google Task to follow up with that lead
  • Else (Junk, No Show, Callback, etc.) → send an email alert so someone reviews it

Part 1 — Build the Google Sheet

  1. Go to sheets.google.com and create a new spreadsheet
  2. Name it: Lead Tracker
  3. In Row 1, add these four column headers exactly as shown:
    A1: Name B1: Status C1: VA Name D1: Date
    The column names are important — ActivePieces will map your data using them.
  4. Add two test rows so the automation has something to process:
    Row 2: Test Lead 1 | Qualified | Sarah | 2026-06-18 Row 3: Test Lead 2 | Junk | Sarah | 2026-06-18

Part 2 — Sign Up for ActivePieces

  1. Go to activepieces.com and click Get started freeNo credit card required. You can sign up with your Google account.
  2. Once you're inside, click New Flow in the top right corner
  3. Give your flow a name: Lead Status Router, then click Create

Part 3 — Set Up the Trigger

  1. Click Select Trigger on the canvas
  2. Search for Google Sheets and select it
  3. Choose New Row as the trigger event
  4. Click Connect next to the Google connection and sign in with your Google account
  5. Under Spreadsheet, find and select your Lead Tracker sheet
  6. Under Sheet Name, select the first tab (usually called Sheet1)
  7. Click Save

Part 4 — Add the Branch (Your If/Else)

  1. Click the + below the trigger step
  2. Search for Branch and select it — in ActivePieces, a Branch is the if/else step
  3. Under Conditions, click Add condition
  4. Set the condition:
    • First field: click the field and select Status from the sheet data on the left
    • Operator: choose Equals
    • Value: type Qualified (match the exact text from your sheet, including capital Q)
  5. Click Save

Part 5 — True Branch: Qualified → Create a Task

  1. Under the True path, click the +
  2. Search for Google Tasks and select Add Task
  3. Connect your Google account if prompted
  4. Fill in the task details:
    • Task List: select My Tasks (the default Google Task list)
    • Title: click in the field, then select Name from the sheet data — type around it so it reads: Follow up with {{Name}}
    • Notes: type: Qualified lead added by {{VA Name}} on {{Date}} — use the data fields for the parts in curly brackets
  5. Click Save

Part 6 — False Branch: Everything Else → Send a Review Email

  1. Under the False path, click the +
  2. Search for Gmail and select Send Email
  3. Connect your Google account if prompted
  4. Fill in the email:
    • To: type your own email address (or a manager's)
    • Subject: Lead marked {{Status}} — needs review
    • Body: {{VA Name}} added a lead that was not marked Qualified. Name: {{Name}}. Status: {{Status}}. Date: {{Date}}.
  5. Click Save

Part 7 — Test and Publish

  1. Click Test Flow at the top of the screen — ActivePieces will run using your two test rows
  2. Check Google Tasks (tasks.google.com) — you should see "Follow up with Test Lead 1" as a new task
  3. Check your Gmail inbox — you should have received the review email about "Test Lead 2 — Junk"
  4. If both worked, click Publish (top right) to turn the flow onOnce published, the flow will trigger automatically every time a new row is added to your Lead Tracker sheet.
What About Other Statuses?

Right now, your False branch catches everything that isn't "Qualified" — including Callback, No Show, Cold, and anything else. That's fine as a starting point. As you grow comfortable with this, you can nest a second Branch inside the False path to handle more statuses individually.

⚡ Practice

Think through the logic for adding more statuses. Write out each one:

  • If Status = "Callback" → do what?
  • If Status = "No Show" → do what?
  • If Status = "Cold" → do what?
Q&A — Common Questions

What if the automation doesn't trigger?

In Make: make sure the scenario is toggled ON (bottom left). On the free plan, it checks every 15 minutes — not instantly. You can also click Run once to force a test check immediately.

In ActivePieces: make sure the flow is Published, not in Draft. Click Publish if you see a blue button at the top.

What if I connected the wrong Google account?

In Make: go to Connections in the left sidebar, find the Google connection, and click Verify or Delete it to reconnect with the right account.

In ActivePieces: click on any Google step, then click the connection dropdown and select Add new connection.

Can I add more than two routes or conditions?

Yes. In Make, click the Router module and add a third route. In ActivePieces, add a second Branch step inside the False path. This is called nested logic and it's how you handle three or more possible outcomes.

What if I make a mistake while building?

Both tools have an undo button (Ctrl+Z in Make, and a back arrow in ActivePieces). You can also delete any step by right-clicking it and selecting Delete. Nothing is permanent until you turn the automation ON or Publish it.
