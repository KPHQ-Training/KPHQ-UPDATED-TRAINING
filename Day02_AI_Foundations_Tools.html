Prompting Foundations II: Power Blocks

Yesterday you learned the first three blocks: Role, Task, Context. Today you add the three that turn "pretty good" output into reliable, ready-to-use work: Constraints, Examples, and Format.

20-second recap of Day 1

Role (who it should be) plus Task (what to do) plus Context (the background a new hire would need). That is the base. Now we bolt on the parts that control quality.

Block 4 - Constraints: set the guardrails

Constraints tell the model what to avoid and what rules to follow. They head off the rambling, the wrong tone, and the wall of text before it happens, instead of you fixing it after.

Constraint typeExample you can add
Length"Under 120 words." "Exactly 3 bullet points."
What to avoid"No jargon. No emojis. Do not invent statistics."
Must include"Mention the free tune-up offer and our Friday deadline."
Reading level / tone"Plain English a busy homeowner can skim. Warm, not salesy."
No constraints
"Write a follow-up email." You get 400 hedged words, three emojis, and a tone that is off for the client.
Constrained
"Write a follow-up email under 90 words, warm and direct, no emojis, ending in one clear question." You get something you can send.

Block 5 - Examples: show, do not just tell

One or two examples of what "good" looks like beats a paragraph describing it. Giving the model a sample to copy is called few-shot prompting, and it is the fastest way to lock in a format, voice, or style.

Zero-shot (just asking)
"Write product taglines for our cleaning service." The style is a coin toss and rarely matches your brand.
Few-shot (with examples)
"Match the style of these two: 'Spotless, every time.' / 'We sweat the small stuff.' Now write 5 more." The output snaps to your voice.
The Principle

When words fail to describe what you want, show an example. The model is a world-class imitator. Give it the right thing to imitate.

Day 2 · Session 2

Format & the Full Blueprint

The last block, then we assemble all six into one prompt you could hand to a capable new hire and trust the result.

Block 6 - Format: specify the shape of the output

Tell it exactly how to deliver: bullets, a table, a word count, a JSON object, or an email with a subject line. Format-locked output is output you can paste straight into the doc, the CRM, or the send box.

Loose
"Summarize this call." You get a shapeless paragraph you still have to reorganize.
Format-locked
"Summarize as: 1 line on the main pain point, 3 bullets on next steps, and a one-line recommended reply." Drop-in ready.

The full prompt blueprint

Stack all six blocks and you go from a vague wish to a brief someone could actually execute. You will not need all six every time, but knowing them means you always know what to add when output is weak.

Role · Task · Context · Constraints · Examples · FormatYou are a social media manager for a boutique real-estate brokerage. (Role) Write Instagram captions announcing a new listing. (Task) Context: a 2-bedroom condo in a walkable neighborhood; audience is first-time buyers aged 28 to 40. (Context) Constraints: under 60 words each, warm and confident, no hashtags stuffed at the end, do not invent details about the unit. (Constraints) Match the style of this caption: "Light, space, and a commute you will actually enjoy." (Example) Format: give me 3 options as a numbered list. (Format)
The Principle

A weak prompt is a wish. A strong prompt is a brief. The six blocks are your checklist for turning one into the other.

Day 2 · Prompting, Leveled Up

From Prompting to Context Engineering

You now have the 6-block blueprint. Here is where it goes next. The strongest operators spend less time hunting for the perfect sentence and more time shaping everything around the request. That shift has a name, and three habits get you there.

Meta prompting: use AI to write your prompts

Stop writing prompts from scratch. Ask Claude to write or upgrade the prompt for you, then save it.

"Write me a prompt that gets the best possible weekly client report from these notes. Ask me anything you need first."

Why it matters: your prompts improve fast, and you build a reusable Prompt Library across clients.

Cross-referencing: give it your sources, not just instructions

Instead of trusting what the model already knows, hand it the real material (the brief, the transcript, the sheet) and have it reconcile and cite.

"Using the call transcript and the pricing sheet I pasted, draft the follow-up. Flag anything the two sources disagree on."

Why it matters: answers get grounded in your facts, and hallucinations drop because it is working from your material.

The shift

A prompt is one message. Context engineering is the whole setup: what to include, what to leave out, which sources to attach, what examples to show, and which Project or system prompt to work inside. The question stopped being "what do I type?" and became "what does the AI need in front of it to do this well?" We go deep on it tomorrow in Day 3.

Debugging Bad Output

When AI gives you something weak, it is rarely "the AI being dumb." It is almost always a fixable gap in the prompt. Here is how an operator diagnoses and repairs it.

The mindset shift

Bad output is not a dead end, it is feedback. The model did exactly what your prompt implied. So the question is never "why is it wrong," it is "what did my prompt leave open?" Fix the input, not your mood.

The four common failure modes

Almost every weak result is one of these four. Learn the symptom, the cause, and the one-move fix.

SymptomReal causeThe fix
Too generic / blandNo Role, no Context, no examplesAdd who it is for and a sample of "good"
Wrong or made-up factsYou asked from memory; it filled gapsPaste the source material; tell it not to invent
Wrong format or toneYou never specified eitherAdd Format and a tone Constraint
Ignored an instructionBuried in a long, messy promptPut key rules on their own lines; ask for one thing

Notice the pattern: every fix is just adding back one of the six blocks. Debugging is not a new skill, it is the blocks in reverse.

The debug loop: change one thing at a time

Do not rewrite the whole prompt and re-roll the dice. Change one variable, re-run, and compare. That is how you learn what actually moved the result, and how you reach "good" in two or three tries instead of ten.

1
Diagnose
which of the 4 failure modes is it?
2
Change one block
add the missing piece only
3
Re-run
generate again
4
Compare
better? keep going. worse? revert
The Principle

Prompting is a conversation, not a slot machine. Iterate one change at a time and the model becomes predictable.

The Right Tool

There is no single "best AI." There is the right category of tool for the job in front of you. An operator picks deliberately instead of forcing one tool to do everything.

The Principle

Match the job to the category, then use whatever your client or team has standardized on. The skill is the choice, not the brand.

The 2026 landscape, by category

Tools change every month, so do not memorize brands. Learn the categories and what each is built for. Most names below overlap into several categories, which is exactly why you choose by the job.

CategoryExamplesReach for it when
Conversational assistantChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, CopilotDrafting, rewriting, reasoning, general Q&A
Web-grounded researchPerplexity, Gemini, ChatGPT searchYou need current info with sources you can check
Media generationImage, video, and voice toolsYou need a picture, video, or audio, not text
Embedded / in-app AIAI inside GoHighLevel, Canva, Notion, your CRMThe work already lives in that tool

No category wins outright. The fastest operators keep two or three they trust and switch based on the task, not loyalty.

Chat vs Automation vs Agent

Beyond which brand, decide how the AI should run. This is the choice that separates someone who saves minutes from someone who saves hours.

ModeWhat it isBest for
ChatYou prompt, it answers, you stay in the loopOne-off, creative, or judgment-heavy tasks
AutomationA trigger runs fixed steps with no one watchingRepeatable work on a schedule or event
AgentAI plans multiple steps and uses tools to reach a goalMulti-step tasks that need decisions along the way

Rule of thumb: if you have done it the same way three times, it probably belongs in an automation, not a chat. We build those starting Day 4.

The decision framework

Before you open a tool, ask these in order. The first "yes" points you to the right category.

Ask yourselfIf yes, reach for
Does it involve regulated or client-private data?An approved, contracted environment only (never a free public tool)
Do I need current info or facts I can cite?A web-grounded research tool
Do I need an image, video, or voice?A media generation tool
Does this repeat on a trigger or schedule?An automation
Is the work already inside one platform?That platform's embedded AI
None of the above, I just need to think or draft?A conversational assistant
Carry-over from Day 1

The data-safety question comes first, every time. The right tool is never worth leaking a client's regulated data.

Make the most of free tiers

Free does not mean limited if you use it well. These habits stretch a zero-budget stack across a full day of real work.

Five budget habits

1. Spread the load. Each tool has its own daily limit. Keep two or three open and rotate when one slows down.

2. Start fresh. A new chat resets the context, so begin a new one per task instead of one endless thread.

3. Match the tool to the job. Do not burn the heaviest model on a simple rewrite. Save the strongest one for the hard task.

4. Reuse your prompts. Keep the prompt library from earlier today so you are not re-typing the same setup every time.

5. Switch, do not pay. When a free limit hits, move to the next tool. You rarely need a subscription to finish the day.
